## CRITICAL UPFRONT: WHY THIS CATEGORY BITES NEW OPERATORS

Depression glass is the single most mishandled category at estate sales. Here is why:

**Problem 1 — It all looks similar from 3 feet away.** Pink plate is a pink plate until you identify the pattern and color precisely — and then it's either a $6 item or a $180 item.

**Problem 2 — Color matters more than pattern in many cases.** The same pattern in amber might be worth $8. In the right rare blue it might be worth $200. Operators who ignore color tier lose money constantly.

**Problem 3 — Condition sensitivity is brutal.** A chip the size of a pencil eraser destroys 60–80% of collector value. Cloudiness from dishwasher use is permanent and irreversible. You must assess condition on every piece individually.

**Problem 4 — Reproductions are everywhere in the most popular patterns.** Cherry Blossom, Madrid, Mayfair, and Royal Lace have all been heavily reproduced. Pricing a reproduction as original is the most common expensive mistake in this category. **[REV]** The reverse mistake — rejecting an authentic piece using a wrong reproduction "test" — is equally damaging. Use only NDGA-verified authentication tests (see Section 9).

**Problem 5 — This category rewards set completion dramatically.** Four matching tumblers in excellent condition are worth 3–4x what four individual tumblers are worth. Always look for sets.

## SECTION 1: WHAT IS DEPRESSION GLASS — THE BOUNDARIES

You must know what depression glass IS and what it IS NOT, because related categories (Elegant glass, Carnival glass, Jadeite kitchenware, Fire-King) are frequently confused with it and have different valuation logic.

**Depression glass IS:**
- Machine-pressed, transparent or translucent colored glassware
- Manufactured primarily 1929–1939, some production through early 1940s
- Made by approximately 20 American manufacturers, primarily in the Ohio River Valley
- 100+ documented patterns
- Originally cheap or free — given away as premiums with flour, cereal, at movie theaters, or as gas station giveaways
- Identifiable by: mold seam lines, tiny air bubbles, slightly uneven color, pattern pressed INTO or raised FROM the glass

**Depression glass is NOT:**
- **Elegant glass** — hand-finished, etched, higher quality (Fostoria, Cambridge, Heisey, Tiffin). Different category, often more valuable, different identification rules. If the pattern is ETCHED rather than pressed, it is almost certainly Elegant glass, not Depression glass.
- **Carnival glass** — iridescent, multicolored surface sheen. Depression glass has no iridescence. If it shimmers like oil on water, it's Carnival glass.
- **Jadeite kitchenware** — Fire-King Jadeite (Anchor Hocking, 1945–1976) is related but distinct from Depression-era glass. Massive separate collector market.
- **Milk glass** — opaque white, different manufacturing process and collector base.
- **Cut glass** — hand-cut patterns, heavy, brilliant — entirely different category, far older.

**When uncertain whether a piece is Depression or Elegant glass:**
- Depression glass: pattern is MOLDED/PRESSED — you can feel it raised or recessed on the surface
- Elegant glass: pattern is ETCHED or CUT — looks like it was scratched or carved into the surface after forming

This single test eliminates most confusion.

## SECTION 2: 60-SECOND FIELD TRIAGE

Walk up to any glass piece. Do this in order:

**Step 1 — Color identification (5 seconds)**
What color is this? (see Section 3 for color tier system)
Color determines your urgency level — cobalt blue, red, and rare colors trigger immediate closer inspection.

**Step 2 — Hold to light (10 seconds)**
Look for: tiny air bubbles trapped in the glass (authentic), mold seam lines running vertically (authentic), uneven color distribution (authentic).
A perfectly uniform piece with no bubbles and no seams is a red flag for reproduction.

**Step 3 — Pattern identification (20 seconds)**
Look at the primary design: floral, geometric, or figural?
(see Section 4 for pattern identification guide)

**Step 4 — Condition check (15 seconds)**
- Run fingertip around entire rim — feel for chips
- Check center of plates for utensil scratches (parallel scratches = heavy use)
- Hold to light at angle — look for cracks, chips, repairs
- Check for cloudiness (milky haze from dishwasher use — permanent, irreversible)

**Step 5 — Look for sets**
Are there matching pieces nearby? Sets multiply value 3–4x. Always group and sell as sets.

**Step 6 — Price and channel decision** (see Section 5 and 6)

## SECTION 3: COLOR IDENTIFICATION AND VALUE TIERS

**Color is the single most important value driver in Depression glass.** Pattern matters. Piece type matters. But the wrong color in the right pattern is worth a fraction of the right color in the same pattern.

Memorize this tier system. It will save you from the most expensive mistakes.

---

### TIER 1 — MAXIMUM VALUE (always stop and assess carefully)

**Cobalt Blue**
Deep, rich royal blue. Made using cobalt oxide. One of the rarest production colors.
**[REV] Critical:** Cobalt blue is the signature color of **Royal Lace** (Hazel-Atlas) and **Moderntone** (Hazel-Atlas). It is **NOT** an original Hocking Mayfair color — any "cobalt blue Mayfair" is a reproduction (see Section 9).
Royal Lace cobalt blue cookie jar with lid (authentic): $400–$900+ depending on condition (down from historical $700+ peak; recent dealer asking prices Replacements.com / Ruby Lane $300–$800 range)
Royal Lace cobalt blue pitcher: $150–$300
Any cobalt blue piece in any pattern: stop and price carefully.
Reproduction risk: significant — Royal Lace cookie jars have been reproduced; check pattern sharpness.

**Ruby Red / Royal Ruby**
Deep, rich red. Rarer than most colors. Made using gold-based formulas (expensive to produce, hence limited production).
Any red piece in a documented Depression pattern: significant premium.
Distinguish from later Anchor Hocking Royal Ruby (1938–1960s and 1977 reissues) — later pieces are still collectible but at a discount.

**Alexandrite**
Extremely rare color-changing glass — appears blue-lavender in daylight, pink-purple under incandescent light.
Almost never seen at estate sales. If you think you have it: photograph in both natural and artificial light and escalate.
Value: $200–$1,000+ per piece depending on pattern and form.

**Amethyst / Purple**
Rich purple. Limited production. Strong collector demand.
Not common — if you see true purple Depression glass (not pink, not lavender, but purple), treat it as high-value. **[REV]** Royal Lace rolled-edge candlesticks were also made in amethyst — one of the rarest documented Royal Lace pieces.

**Tangerine / Orange**
Very limited production runs. Extremely rare at estate sales.
If you see bright orange Depression glass: escalate before pricing.

**[REV] Hocking Mayfair "Blue" (unique color)**
A special soft, almost-turquoise blue developed by Hocking ONLY for the Mayfair line — more vibrant than sapphire, softer than cobalt. **Not cobalt blue.** Per JustGlass.com: "There's nothing quite like it in any other depression pattern and collectors grab whatever blue items they can find, almost regardless of the price."
Mayfair blue cookie jar (authentic): historically $1,500–$3,000+ at NDGA-tier shows. **Current market is softer** — Mayfair blue cookie jar with lid is reaching $800–$1,800 at most recent auctions (auction hammers 2024–2025 confirm range, exclusive of buyer's premium).
Mayfair blue footed tumbler: $400–$700 each (down from historical $720+ peak).
Mayfair blue pitcher (80 oz): $500–$900.
**This is a different blue from Royal Lace cobalt — they should not be conflated.**

### TIER 2 — HIGH VALUE (look more carefully, price thoughtfully)

**Uranium / Vaseline Glass**
Yellowish-green color containing trace uranium oxide. Glows bright green under UV/black light.
**Carry a small UV flashlight to every sale.** Any glass that glows under UV is uranium glass and warrants careful pricing.
Safe to handle and display; some collectors prefer display-only rather than food use.
Value premium: 50–150% above the same pattern in non-uranium colors.
Uranium green is DIFFERENT from standard Depression green — the glow test distinguishes them.
**[REV]** Caution: some modern green Depression-era reproductions also fluoresce under UV. The glow test confirms uranium content but does NOT confirm authenticity. Combine with pattern sharpness and weight tests.

**Jadeite (opaque milky green)**
Opaque, milky, jade-colored glass. Made primarily by Jeannette Glass.
Separate from transparent green Depression glass — the opacity is distinctive.
Massive collector following, especially for kitchenware forms (canisters, bowls, mugs).
Fire-King Jadeite (post-1945): different but also highly collected. Do not conflate.
Value: $30–$200+ per piece depending on form and rarity.

**Delphite (opaque pale blue)**
Soft, powder-blue opaque glass. Made primarily by Jeannette.
Less common than transparent colors. Distinct collector following.
Cherry Blossom in Delphite: significant premium over pink or green.
Value: treat as Tier 2, 40–80% premium over equivalent pink piece.

**Black / Ebony**
Opaque black glass. Very limited production. Strong collector demand.
If you see matte or glossy black Depression-era glass: treat as high-value, check pattern.

**Monax (opalescent white)**
Made primarily by MacBeth-Evans for American Sweetheart pattern.
Translucent white with slight blue-white opalescence at edges.
American Sweetheart in Monax: strong collector market.
Distinguish from later milk glass — Monax is thinner and slightly translucent.

### TIER 3 — MODERATE VALUE (standard Depression glass collector market)

**Pink**
The most popular and most produced color. Most collector demand by volume.
Dusty rose / soft rose tone — made using selenium oxide.
REPRODUCTION ALERT: Original pink is dusty, muted rose. Reproduction pink is brighter, more bubblegum or orange-pink. If the pink looks too vivid or "new," it may be a reproduction.
Value: standard collector pricing (see Section 5 pattern tables).

**Green (transparent)**
Second most produced color. Iron oxide gives the pale green tone.
Common but still collectible. Value typically 70–90% of equivalent pink piece.
Note: uranium green glows under UV; standard green does not. This distinction can double the value.

**Ultramarine**
Blue-green / teal color. Limited production. Higher premium than standard green or pink.
Jeannette made Swirl pattern in ultramarine — collectible. Value: 50–100% premium over pink. **[REV]** Doc previously credited Anchor Hocking — actually Jeannette.

**Ice Blue / Pale Blue (transparent)**
Very pale, almost colorless blue. Mayfair in Ice Blue is a known premium variant.
Do not confuse with later blue glass — Depression-era ice blue is very delicate in tone.

---

### TIER 4 — LOW TO MODERATE VALUE (common, still sells)

**Amber / Golden**
The first popular Depression glass color — and the most produced. Flooded the market.
Common amber in common patterns: $3–$15 per piece. Rarely worth online listing effort.
Exception: amber in rare patterns or rare forms (pitchers, cookie jars) — still check.

**Crystal / Clear**
Machine-pressed clear glass. Lowest collector demand of any color.
Price as decorative glassware: $2–$8 per piece unless pattern is documented and rare form.

**Canary / Yellow (pale)**
Pale yellow. More collectible than amber but still common.
Value: 20–40% premium over amber in equivalent pattern.

### COLOR IDENTIFICATION QUICK TEST

**Is it pink or red?**
Hold up to bright light. Pink is translucent, you can see through it clearly. True ruby/red is much darker and more opaque-feeling.

**Is it green or uranium green?**
Hold UV flashlight 6 inches from piece in dim light. Uranium glass glows bright, almost neon green. Regular green does not glow.

**Is it jadeite or mint green transparent?**
Jadeite is opaque — you cannot see light through it. Transparent green Depression glass lets light through.

**Is it delphite or pale blue transparent?**
Delphite is opaque like jadeite — you cannot see light through it. Ice blue lets light through.

**[REV] Is it cobalt blue or Hocking Mayfair blue?**
Hold both colors side-by-side if possible. Cobalt is deep, dark, royal — light barely penetrates. Mayfair blue is lighter, slightly turquoise, almost translucent. If you have a piece in deep cobalt with the Mayfair Open Rose pattern, **it is a reproduction** (Hocking never made Mayfair in cobalt).

## SECTION 4: MANUFACTURER IDENTIFICATION & THEIR KEY PATTERNS

Most Depression glass is NOT marked with a maker's name on the piece itself. Manufacturer identification comes from pattern recognition cross-referenced with known production records.

**The authoritative reference:** Gene Florence's *Collector's Encyclopedia of Depression Glass* (updated editions through 2022). If you buy one book for this category, this is it. ~$25–$30 on Amazon.

---

### THE MAJOR MANUFACTURERS

**[REV] HOCKING GLASS COMPANY → ANCHOR HOCKING GLASS CORPORATION**
*Lancaster, Ohio — became Anchor Hocking after merger with Anchor Cap Corporation in 1937*
**Important distinction:** Many key Depression-era patterns (Mayfair, Cameo, Princess, Miss America) were produced by **Hocking Glass** before the 1937 merger. Pieces from 1931–1937 are technically Hocking, not Anchor Hocking. For pricing purposes treat identically; for accurate identification cite Hocking for pre-1937 patterns.
Mark: Distinctive "H" over anchor on base (post-1937; pre-merger pieces unmarked or marked differently)
Key patterns: Mayfair (Open Rose, 1931–1937), Cameo (Ballerina, 1930–1934), Princess (1931–1935), Miss America (1935–1936), Block Optic, Bubble (Anchor Hocking, 1934–1965), Florentine No. 1, Colonial (Knife & Fork), Waterford (Waffle)
Known for: High volume production, wide color range, strong pattern variety
Mayfair is their most collected and most valuable pattern.

**FEDERAL GLASS COMPANY**
*Columbus, Ohio*
Mark: Shield mark or "F" in a shield (inconsistently applied)
Key patterns: Sharon (Cabbage Rose), Madrid, Patrician (Speaking of Wheat), Columbia, Normandie
Known for: Sharon (one of the most collected patterns), Madrid (frequently reproduced in 1970s)
Federal's amber is some of the most common Depression glass found at estate sales — price accordingly.

**HAZEL-ATLAS GLASS COMPANY**
*Clarksburg, West Virginia*
Mark: "H" over "A" on base (look carefully — easy to miss)
Key patterns: Royal Lace, Moderntone, Florentine No. 2, Newport (Hairpin), Cloverleaf, Aurora
Known for: Royal Lace in cobalt blue — the most valuable Hazel-Atlas pattern by far
Moderntone in cobalt blue is their second most valuable pattern.

**JEANNETTE GLASS COMPANY**
*Jeannette, Pennsylvania*
Mark: Usually unmarked
Key patterns: Cherry Blossom, Adam, Windsor Diamond, Sunflower, Iris and Herringbone, Cubist, Floral (Poinsettia), Swirl (Ultramarine)
Known for: Cherry Blossom — most heavily reproduced Depression glass pattern in existence
Also known for Jadeite and Delphite production — opaque colors that command premiums.

**MACBETH-EVANS GLASS COMPANY**
*Charleroi, Pennsylvania (acquired by Corning 1936)* **[REV]** Year corrected to 1936
Mark: Occasionally "MAC" or "M" in a circle
Key patterns: American Sweetheart (1930–1936), Dogwood (Apple Blossom), Petalware, Thistle, S-Pattern (Stippled Rose Band)
Known for: Thin, delicate glass; American Sweetheart in Monax is their signature piece
American Sweetheart water pitcher in pink: $260–$895 depending on condition and rarity.

**INDIANA GLASS COMPANY**
*Dunkirk, Indiana*
Mark: Rarely marked
Key patterns: Horseshoe (No. 612), Sandwich, Lorain (Basket or Number 615), Avocado
Known for: Reissued several patterns in 1970s–80s (Madrid as "Recollection") — creates reproduction identification challenges
Indiana Glass continued production long after other Depression-era manufacturers — later pieces are less collectible.

## SECTION 5: PATTERN IDENTIFICATION — THE TOP 15

These are the patterns you will encounter most frequently and that carry the most value risk if misidentified. Learn these first.

**Identification method:** Look at the primary design motif, then the rim treatment, then the manufacturer's known colors. Cross-reference with Gene Florence photographs or the NDGA pattern guide at ndga.net.

### TIER A — HIGHEST VALUE PATTERNS (always investigate carefully)

**ROYAL LACE** — Hazel-Atlas Glass, 1934–1941
*The most sought-after pattern in Depression glass.*
Identification: Intricate lacy design with rolling curves, scrolls, and florals — looks "too fancy to be a giveaway." Ruffled or straight edges on serving pieces.
Colors made: Cobalt blue, pink, green, crystal, amethyst (rolled-edge candlesticks only — rarest) **[REV] amethyst added**
The cobalt blue: this is where the serious money lives.
**[REV] Updated pricing based on 2024–2025 sold comps:**
- Cobalt blue Royal Lace pitcher (48 oz): $150–$300
- Cobalt blue Royal Lace cookie jar with lid: $400–$900 (historical peak $700–$1,000 has softened)
- Pink/Green common pieces: $15–$60 each
Reproduction risk: cookie jar has been reproduced; check pattern sharpness, weight, and the inside lid mold lines.

**MAYFAIR (OPEN ROSE)** — Hocking Glass, 1931–1937
*The most valuable "common" pattern.*
Identification: Bold open rose design in center panel with vertical rounded panels on either side. Distinctive scalloped edges on many pieces. Cookie jar is the most recognizable piece in the pattern.
**[REV] Colors made (per JustGlass.com, DepressionElegantGlass.com):**
- **Pink** — most common, full production line
- **Blue** — Hocking's signature soft turquoise-blue (NOT cobalt), most pieces made, very valuable
- **Green** — limited production, mostly handled servers; scarce in other forms
- **Yellow/Topaz** — limited production
- **Crystal** — very limited
- Pink satinized pieces with painted decorations also exist
Pink Mayfair covered cookie jar, excellent condition: $60–$130 (down from historical $80–$150)
Blue Mayfair covered cookie jar (authentic): $800–$1,800 current market (historical $1,500–$3,000+)
Blue Mayfair footed tumbler: $400–$700 each
**[REV] REPRODUCTION ALERT — KEY:** Per RealOrRepro.com and JustGlass.com, Mayfair has been reproduced since 1977 in pink, green, **amethyst, cobalt blue, and red** — colors that **Hocking never made in original production**. Specifically:
- **Original cookie jar knobs are 8-sided** (4 long sides + 4 short connector lines); reproduction knobs are only 6-sided
- **Authentic cookie jar lids** have fine, deeply pressed scalloped edge detail; reproductions softer
- **Authentic pitcher handles** have a distinct "hook" shape; reproductions are rounded
- **Any Mayfair in cobalt blue, amethyst, or red = reproduction** (the "pretty" colors that look most valuable are the fakes)
- Reproductions limited to 4 forms: cookie jar, shot glass, shakers, juice pitcher

**AMERICAN SWEETHEART** — MacBeth-Evans, 1930–1936
Identification: Thin, delicate glass. Ribbed and patterned rim — the rim design is the key identifier (concentric ribbing plus floral elements). Very thin walls compared to most Depression glass.
Colors made: Pink (most common), Monax (opalescent white, very collectible), Red (rare and extremely valuable), Smoke, Cremax
Pink American Sweetheart water pitcher (rare): $260–$895 depending on condition
Monax common pieces (plates, cups): $10–$45 per piece
Red American Sweetheart creamer and sugar: ~$400 for the pair
Lamp shades were made in this pattern and are rare: $400–$700
Reproduction risk: lower than Cherry Blossom or Madrid — but confirm delicate thinness of genuine MacBeth-Evans glass

**CHERRY BLOSSOM** — Jeannette Glass, 1930–1939
*The most heavily reproduced Depression glass pattern in existence.*
Identification: Sprawling branches with five-petaled cherry blossoms and cherry clusters. Busy, all-over pattern covering most of the piece. Scalloped and patterned rims on most pieces.
**[REV] Colors made (per NDGA, JustGlass):** Pink (full production), Green (discontinued 1935), Delphite (opaque blue, added 1936 — significant premium), Crystal (limited), Jadite (experimental/rare), Red (experimental, very rare), Yellow (experimental), Amberina (experimental). The opaque/experimental colors are "leftover glass at end of day" pieces and warrant escalation if claimed authentic.
Common pieces (cups, saucers, plates) in pink/green: $8–$25 each
Delphite Cherry Blossom: 2–3x premium over pink
Child's butter dish in any color: **NEVER PRODUCED BY JEANNETTE** — see authentication note below

**[REV] REPRODUCTION ALERT — MAJOR REWRITE FROM v1.0:**
The "2 vs 3 cherry bumps" test cited in v1.0 is **not supported by NDGA, JustGlass, WorthPoint, or any authoritative source.** Use the NDGA-authoritative tests below instead.

**The authoritative Cherry Blossom authentication tests** (per NDGA's Cathy Trietsch, JustGlass.com, RealOrRepro.com, WorthPoint Worthologist Linda Carannante):

1. **Child's butter dish DOES NOT EXIST in authentic Jeannette production** — Jeannette never made a child's butter dish. Any child's Cherry Blossom butter dish is a 1973+ reproduction (nicknamed "the Cherry Bomb"). The original v1.0 claim that "child's butter dish $400–$800+" is **incorrect for authentic pieces — these are all reproductions.**

2. **Pitcher base test:** Authentic pitcher has **9 cherries** on the base, with leaves/cherries/branch fully filling the base. Any other cherry count = reproduction. New pitchers have a "solid bar of glass" instead of a branch with cherries, and the background is rough rather than smooth.

3. **Butter dish base test:** Authentic base has the pattern (leaves, cherries, branch tips) extending **all the way to the edge** of the base. Reproduction leaves approximately 1/4" plain border around the edge.

4. **Butter dish lid test:** Authentic has **two mold lines visible** around the base of the lid; reproduction has **only one mold line**.

5. **Tumbler test:** Authentic tumblers have **three thin glass rings** separating the patterned section from the plain area near the top. Missing rings = reproduction.

6. **Pattern realism:** Authentic pattern is lifelike, defined, with shaded cherries. Reproduction cherries look "squished" and like circles; leaves look hand-drawn.

7. **Colors that confirm reproduction:** Cobalt blue, red, iridized, and any colors not in the original Jeannette production palette.

8. **Pattern completeness:** Fakes have missing pattern at bottoms of tumblers, cups, and pitchers.

9. **Oily feel:** Some reproductions have silicone mold release residue — slippery feel even after washing.

**Authentic Cherry Blossom child's pieces that DID exist (no butter dish):** cup, saucer, 6-inch plate, sugar, creamer. **Authentic shaker production:** only 2 pairs of pink and ~12 green pairs were verified original — virtually all Cherry Blossom shakers on the market are reproductions worth ~$25, NOT the $800 some sellers ask.

### TIER B — HIGH VALUE PATTERNS

**SHARON (CABBAGE ROSE)** — Federal Glass, 1935–1939
Identification: All-over repeating rose design, slightly off-center placement is characteristic. Distinctive because the roses are fuller and rounder than Mayfair — more "garden" than "bouquet." Chip-mould process gives slight texture difference from most Depression glass.
Colors made: Pink (most common), Amber, Green, Crystal
Pink Sharon cheese dish with lid: ~$200–$280
Pink Sharon set of 4 iced tea tumblers: ~$250–$320
Sharon creamer and sugar reproductions exist — easy to spot: reproductions are heavier and the rose detail is softer.

**CAMEO (BALLERINA)** — Hocking Glass, 1930–1934 **[REV] Hocking, not Anchor/Hocking**
Identification: The ONLY Depression glass pattern featuring a human figure — a dancing girl (ballerina) in a medallion. The most pieces in any single pattern (68 documented pieces). Pattern also includes festoons and scrollwork around the figure.
Colors made: Green (most common), Pink, Yellow, Crystal
Cookie jar value: $80–$150 in green
Children's miniature Cameo was made by Mosser Glass as "Jennifer" — sold as toys, not vintage Depression glass. Do not mistake for original.
Reproduction risk: low for most pieces, but miniature versions are often confused.

**MISS AMERICA** — Hocking Glass, 1935–1936
Identification: Intricate diamond-cut sunburst design radiating from center. Very geometric — no florals. Often mistaken for cut glass but is pressed glass.
Colors made: Crystal (most common), Pink, Green, Ice Blue, Royal Ruby (red)
Pink Miss America candy jar with lid: ~$150–$200
Pink Miss America water pitcher: $100–$200
Reproductions of the pink butter dish exist and are common.

**PRINCESS** — Hocking Glass, 1931–1935
Identification: Structured geometric design with angular lines and Art Deco character. Floral elements present but subordinate to the geometric framework. Scalloped rim on most pieces.
Colors made: Green (most common), Pink, Yellow/Topaz, Apricot/Amber
Green or pink: standard collector pricing
Yellow/Topaz Princess: 30–50% premium over green

**ADAM** — Jeannette Glass, 1932–1934
Identification: Combination of geometric and floral. Square-shaped plates distinguish it from most round Depression glass.
Colors made: Pink, Green, Crystal, Yellow (rare)
Adam covered butter dish: $75–$150

**FLORENTINE NO. 1 AND NO. 2**
Two distinct patterns by two different manufacturers. Easy confusion point:
- Florentine No. 1: Hocking Glass, hexagonal plates **[REV] Hocking not Anchor Hocking — pattern was pre-1937**
- Florentine No. 2: Hazel-Atlas, round plates
Both feature similar floral and scroll border designs. Identifying which you have matters for pattern completeness matching.

### TIER C — MODERATE VALUE PATTERNS (common, steady collector market)

**MADRID** — Federal Glass, 1932–1939
Identification: Strong geometric repeating motif with somewhat bold, modern feel compared to soft florals of other patterns. Square serving pieces available.
Colors made: Amber (most common and least valuable), Pink, Green, Blue (rare), Crystal
**REPRODUCTION ALERT:** Indiana Glass reproduced Madrid in 1976–1977. These authorized reproductions are marked with "1976" or "1977" on the base. Pre-1939 originals have NO date mark. This is the most reliable Madrid authentication test. Indiana called their version "Recollection" — some pieces made in teal and other colors that were NEVER in the original Federal production run.

**MODERNTONE** — Hazel-Atlas, 1934–1942 (continued into 1950s as "Platonite")
Identification: Plain concentric rib pattern — simple, utilitarian design. Distinctive because it's LESS ornate than most Depression glass.
Colors made: Cobalt blue (most collected), Amethyst, Pink, Crystal, and later Platonite (fired-on colors)
Cobalt blue Moderntone: moderate premium — $15–$45 per piece
Amethyst: similar premium to cobalt

**WINDSOR DIAMOND** — Jeannette Glass, 1936–1946
Identification: Diamond-point pattern all over the piece, creating a quilted or crosshatch appearance. Sometimes called simply "Windsor."
Colors made: Pink, Green, Crystal, Amber, Red (rare)
Common pink/green pieces: $8–$25

**BUBBLE** — Anchor Hocking, 1934–1965 (long production run)
Identification: All-over pattern of raised bumps or "bubbles." Made for a very long time — later pieces less valuable.
Colors made: Crystal, Pink, Green, Royal Ruby (red), Sapphire Blue
Long production run means condition and color matter even more than usual — later pieces are not collectible.

## SECTION 6: CONDITION ASSESSMENT — THE DETAILED CHECKLIST

Depression glass condition sensitivity is extreme. A single chip can destroy 60–80% of collector value. There is no such thing as "minor damage" in the eyes of serious collectors.

### The Four Condition Killers (in order of severity)

**1. CHIPS**
Even tiny chips — edge chips the size of a pinhead — are value-destroying for collector purposes.
Feel, don't just look: run fingertip around every rim and edge. Your finger catches chips that your eye misses.
A chipped piece in any pattern drops from collector value to decorative/use value.
**Decorator/use floor price for chipped Depression glass: $2–$8 per piece regardless of pattern or color.** Exception: cobalt blue or other rare color — even chipped may be worth $15–$30 as color reference.

**2. CLOUDINESS / SICK GLASS**
A milky, hazy, etched appearance on the surface — most often from dishwasher detergent.
Hold to light at an angle — cloudiness is most visible this way.
**Cloudiness is PERMANENT AND IRREVERSIBLE.** Do not suggest to clients that it can be cleaned. It cannot.
Cloudy Depression glass: 50–70% value reduction for collector purposes. Still decorative.
Decoration-only floor price for cloudy pieces: $3–$12.

**3. CRACKS**
True cracks (not mold seam lines, which are authentic and expected) are immediate disqualifiers for collector value.
Light from behind: cracks show as dark lines running through the glass.
Mold seam lines run straight and vertical — they are expected and authentic.
Cracks run irregularly and often have a slightly different surface feel.
Cracked piece: $2–$5 regardless of pattern or color.

**4. UTENSIL SCRATCHES**
Parallel scratches in the center of plates and bowls from fork and knife use.
Light surface scratching: 15–25% value reduction.
Heavy parallel scratching covering center of plate: 40–60% value reduction.
Note: this primarily affects plates and bowls used for actual dining.

### The Condition Grading Scale

| Grade | Description | Multiplier |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Mint | Perfect. No flaws, no wear, no scratches. Rare for age. | 1.3–1.5x |
| Excellent | No chips, cracks, or cloudiness. Minor scratches acceptable. | 1.0x (baseline) |
| Good | Light scratching or minor utensil wear. No chips or cracks. | 0.6–0.8x |
| Fair | Noticeable scratching, minor cloudiness, or small chip | 0.2–0.4x |
| Damaged | Chip, crack, heavy cloudiness, or significant damage | 0.05–0.1x (decorator only) |

### Set Completion Premium

This is the most undervalued concept in Depression glass pricing:

**Individual pieces:** baseline value
**Matched set of 4:** 2.5–3x individual value (buyers want complete sets desperately)
**Matched set of 6:** 3–4x individual value
**Complete service for 8 with serving pieces:** 5–8x individual value

Always inventory the full cabinet or box before pricing any piece. Eight pink Sharon luncheon plates individually might be $8 each ($64 total). As a set of 8 with matching cups and saucers: $180–$250 as a set. Always sell sets together.

## SECTION 7: PIECE TYPE VALUE HIERARCHY

Within any pattern and color, certain forms command dramatic premiums. Know these.

### Highest Premium Forms (2–5x standard piece value)

**Cookie jars with original lid** — Among the most valuable pieces in almost any pattern. The lid must be original, matching, and unchipped. A cookie jar missing its lid: 30% of the value with lid.

**Water pitchers / juice pitchers** — High-demand serving pieces. Mayfair blue pitcher alone: $500–$900.

**Butter dishes with lid** — Must have original matching lid. A butter dish bottom without the lid: 20% of value with lid. **[REV]** Note: Cherry Blossom child's butter dish does not exist as authentic — see Section 5.

**Cake plates / cake stands** — Large, prone to chips, therefore hard to find in excellent condition. Premium for excellent examples.

**Salt and pepper shakers (matched pair)** — Must be original pair. Shakers lose most value if separated. **[REV]** Cherry Blossom shakers: virtually all on market are reproductions; only ~14 authentic pairs documented.

**Refrigerator dishes with lids** — Especially Jadeite and Delphite. Practical + collectible = premium.

**Reamers / citrus juicers** — Any Depression glass juicer in excellent condition commands a strong premium. Forms that were heavily used are now rare in good condition.

**Canisters (with lids)** — Especially in Jadeite and Delphite. Complete canister sets: high premium.

### Standard Value Forms

Dinner plates, luncheon plates, soup bowls, salad bowls, cups, saucers, creamers, sugar bowls — the backbone of most collections. Steady demand, standard pricing.

### Lower Premium Forms (common, lower demand)

Tumblers (unless footed and in rare color), sherbet glasses, berry bowls, cereal bowls — very common, often the pieces that didn't break.

**IMPORTANT EXCEPTION:** Tumblers and footed tumblers in rare colors (Mayfair blue footed tumbler: $400–$700 each) are a dramatic exception to this rule. Color overrides form in Tier 1 colors.

## SECTION 9: REPRODUCTIONS AND FAKES — MANDATORY READING

The four most dangerous patterns for reproductions are Cherry Blossom, Madrid, Mayfair, and Royal Lace. These are also among the most valuable — which is why the reproduction incentive exists.

### Universal Reproduction Tests (apply to all patterns)

**Weight test:** Reproductions are consistently heavier than originals. Depression glass was mass-produced cheaply — thin walls, light weight. If it feels substantial and heavy for its size, be suspicious.

**Pattern sharpness:** Original pressed Depression glass has crisp, sharp pattern detail from precision molds. Reproductions (made from molds copied from originals) have slightly softer, less defined pattern edges.

**Color accuracy:**
- Original pink: dusty rose, muted, soft
- Reproduction pink: brighter, more bubblegum, sometimes slightly orange-pink
- Original green: soft sage/celery tone
- Reproduction green: sometimes too vivid or off-tone

**Seam lines:** Authentic Depression glass has mold seam lines running vertically up the sides — these are expected and authenticate the piece. Absence of seam lines on a piece that should have them is a reproduction flag.

**Air bubbles:** Hold to light. Authentic pieces contain tiny air bubbles from the machine-pressing process. Reproductions often have none (modern manufacturing is cleaner).

**Oily feel:** Some reproduction manufacturers treat molds with silicone release agents. This transfers to the glass as a persistent greasy or oily feel that doesn't wash away. If a piece feels slippery even after washing: reproduction.

---

### Pattern-Specific Reproduction Tests

**[REV] CHERRY BLOSSOM — MAJOR REWRITE:**
The v1.0 "cherry bump count" test is not supported by NDGA, JustGlass, RealOrRepro, or WorthPoint. Use these authoritative tests instead:

1. **Pitcher base: 9 cherries on authentic, with branch pattern filling base.** Reproductions have a "solid bar of glass" instead of a defined branch; pattern doesn't fill base; background rough rather than smooth.

2. **Butter dish base: pattern extends to edge.** Reproductions leave ~1/4" plain border around base.

3. **Butter dish lid: 2 mold lines on authentic; 1 mold line on reproduction.**

4. **Tumblers: 3 thin rings separating patterned area from plain area near top.** Missing rings = reproduction.

5. **Child's butter dish never existed in authentic Jeannette production.** Any child's butter dish = 1973+ "Cherry Bomb" reproduction.

6. **Authentic shakers: only ~14 verified pairs exist.** Virtually all on market = reproductions ($25 value, not $500+).

7. **Colors never produced originally:** cobalt blue, iridized — any Cherry Blossom in these colors is reproduction.

8. **Cup/saucer test:** Authentic stems touch leaves and blossoms; reproduction stems do not connect to leaves/blossoms.

9. **Pattern realism:** Authentic = lifelike, defined, shaded; reproduction = "squished," cartoon-like, hand-drawn appearance.

**MADRID:**
Date mark: Indiana Glass 1976–1977 reproductions (sold as "Recollection") are marked "1976" or "1977" on the base. Pre-1939 originals have NO date mark. Flip the piece over — this test is definitive.
Color tells: Indiana made Recollection in teal and other colors NEVER made by Federal in the original Madrid run. Any Madrid in teal = reproduction.
Feel: experienced collectors report a "different feel" to the Recollection amber vs. original Federal amber — original feels slightly different in hand. This takes experience to develop.

**[REV] MAYFAIR — EXPANDED:**
Per RealOrRepro.com and JustGlass.com:
- **Cookie jar knob: 8 sides on authentic** (4 long sides connected by 4 short lines at corners). Reproduction knobs have only 6 sides. This is the single most reliable Mayfair authentication test.
- **Cookie jar lid scalloping:** original has fine, deeply pressed scalloped edge detail; reproduction softer and less defined.
- **Pitcher handles:** original handles have a distinct "hook" shape at the top; reproduction handles are more rounded.
- **Pink color:** original is muted rose; reproduction pink is too vivid or too orange.
- **Reproduction colors that don't exist in authentic Mayfair:** cobalt blue, amethyst, red. **Any Mayfair in these colors is a reproduction.** This is the most common selling-error trap because these colors look "valuable."
- Reproductions limited to 4 forms: cookie jar, shot glass, shakers, juice pitcher.

**ROYAL LACE:**
Cookie jar has been reproduced. Check weight (reproductions heavier), pattern sharpness, and inside lid mold lines.
Cobalt blue Royal Lace: the cobalt color itself is rarely faked because cobalt production is difficult to match exactly, but reproduction cobalt does exist on the cookie jar specifically.

**SHARON:**
Creamer and sugar reproduced — easy to spot. Reproductions are heavier and rose detail is softer. Compare in hand against a known original if possible.

---

### Indiana Glass Reissues — A Special Problem Category

Indiana Glass reissued multiple Depression glass patterns in the 1970s and 1980s as authorized (not counterfeit) reproductions. These are legitimately sold as vintage glass today and create genuine confusion.

**Patterns reissued by Indiana:**
- Madrid (as "Recollection") — 1976–1977
- Avocado — colors that were never in original run are the tell
- Sandwich — Indiana's version vs. Anchor Hocking's original Sandwich pattern

**The tell for Indiana reissues:** Date marks (Madrid), wrong colors for the period, or subtle design differences documented in the Florence guides. When uncertain: check if the piece/color combination is in the Florence guide under the original manufacturer. If not, it may be a reissue.

## SECTION 11: THE 5 MOST EXPENSIVE MISTAKES IN THIS CATEGORY

**[REV] Mistake 1: Missing a Mayfair blue cookie jar — OR pricing a fake one as real**
An authentic Hocking Mayfair blue cookie jar with original lid sitting in a cabinet at an estate sale. Priced at $45 because it's "blue glass." Actual value: $800–$1,800 current market. The color is the entire story here — and it must be the **soft Hocking turquoise**, not cobalt. A cobalt blue Mayfair cookie jar at the same estate is a 1977+ reproduction worth $30. Check the cookie jar knob: 8 sides = authentic; 6 sides = reproduction.

**[REV] Mistake 2: Using the wrong Cherry Blossom authentication test**
Pricing a "Cherry Blossom" piece based on counting cherry bumps (a test not supported by NDGA). This can result in BOTH rejecting authentic pieces AND accepting reproductions. Use the NDGA tests: pitcher base 9 cherries, butter dish base pattern-to-edge, tumbler 3 rings, child's butter dish always reproduction.

**Mistake 3: Selling a set as individual pieces**
Eight matching pink Sharon luncheon plates priced individually at $8 each = $64.
The same eight plates sold as a set with 8 cups and 8 saucers = $220–$280.
Always inventory the full cabinet before pricing any piece in it.

**Mistake 4: Missing cloudiness under bright light**
A piece of American Sweetheart Monax priced at $35 (excellent condition price) that is actually cloudy from dishwasher use. Actual value: $8–$12 as decorator. Cloudiness only shows at certain angles under bright light — you have to look for it deliberately.

**Mistake 5: Not recognizing uranium glass**
Standard green Depression glass priced at $6 per piece. Uranium green Depression glass (glows under UV) worth $12–$30 per piece — same pattern, same form, roughly 3x the value. Carry a UV flashlight. Test every piece of green glass. The 60 seconds it takes to test 20 pieces of green glass is worth it every time. Caveat: some modern reproductions also fluoresce, so combine glow test with pattern sharpness/weight tests.

## SECTION 13: MARKET CONTEXT (Updated May 2026)

**[REV] The market has stratified further and softened at the top.** The historical peaks documented in 2018-era Florence guides (Mayfair blue cookie jar $1,500-$3,000, Royal Lace cobalt cookie jar $700+) are not consistently being hit at recent 2024–2025 auctions. Current market for top pieces:
- Mayfair blue cookie jar: $800–$1,800 (vs $1,500–$3,000 historical)
- Royal Lace cobalt cookie jar: $400–$900 (vs $700–$1,000 historical)
- Mayfair blue footed tumbler: $400–$700 each (vs $720+ historical)
The high-end remains strong relative to common Depression glass, but the spread between excellent-condition and "good-condition" pieces has widened — buyers are pickier about condition than they were 10 years ago.

**Sets are appreciating faster than individual pieces.** As collectors have aged and collections matured, complete sets in excellent condition are increasingly valuable relative to individual pieces. If you find a complete service for 8, treat it as a top-priority item.

**Reproductions are a growing problem.** Awareness of Depression glass value has driven more sophisticated reproduction and reissue activity since 2015. Authentication discipline is more important now than it was 10 years ago. The cobalt blue / amethyst / red Mayfair reproductions have flooded eBay — many listed as "rare" by sellers who don't know they're reproductions.

**Online has become the dominant market.** The NDGA annual show remains the collector premier event, but eBay and Etsy have made the collector market national. Regional price variations have narrowed as a result — your estate sale floor price should be informed by national eBay sold comps, not local antique mall pricing (which is often out of date).

**The "sick glass" problem is underappreciated.** As Depression glass has gotten more valuable, the pieces that have been sitting in display cabinets (not dishwashers) have become relatively scarce. Cloudiness from dishwasher use is permanent and is reducing the supply of truly excellent pieces. Mint and excellent condition pieces command genuine premiums and will increasingly do so.

**Jadeite has its own ecosystem.** Fire-King Jadeite (Anchor Hocking, 1945–1976) has a massive and somewhat separate collector market from classic Depression glass. If you find a Jadeite kitchen scene — canisters, mixing bowls, mugs, refrigerator dishes — treat it as a mini-category requiring its own research.
