## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

This is a field decision tool, not a collector's encyclopedia. Every section answers one of three questions:
1. **What is this?** (Identification)
2. **What is it worth?** (Pricing by channel)
3. **What do I do with it?** (Floor / Online / Escalate)

When in doubt on any piece over $100 estimated value: **photograph bottom, handle, and cooking surface. Check eBay sold listings for the exact logo + size before tagging.**

---

## SECTION 1: FIRST 60-SECOND TRIAGE

Walk up to any cast iron piece. Do this in order:

**Step 1 — Flip it over immediately.**
The bottom tells you almost everything. You need to see: maker name, logo style, size number, pattern number, and country of origin marking.

**Step 2 — Run your hand across the cooking surface.**
Authentic vintage Griswold: glassy smooth, almost silk-like.
Modern or fake: bumpy, pebbly texture like sandpaper.
This one physical test eliminates most fakes in 3 seconds.

**Step 3 — Check for cracks.**
Hold the piece up to light and look across the cooking surface at an angle. Run your finger along the rim. A hairline crack destroys collector value entirely — drops the piece to cookware-only value (typically $5–$15 regardless of maker).

**Step 4 — Check for spinning/rocking.**
Place flat on a hard surface. A piece that rocks or spins has a warped base. Reduces value 40–60% for collector purposes. Still usable cookware.

**Step 5 — Identify the maker** (see Section 2).

**Step 6 — Identify the logo era** (see Section 3 for Griswold, Section 4 for Wagner).

**Step 7 — Price it** (see Section 5).

## SECTION 2: MAKER IDENTIFICATION — WHO MADE THIS

### TIER 1 — HIGH COLLECTOR VALUE (always investigate further)

**GRISWOLD** (Erie, PA | 1865–1957)
The most valuable and most collected American cast iron brand. Look for:
- "GRISWOLD" cast in raised letters, usually inside a cross-and-circle design
- "ERIE, PA U.S.A." on most pieces
- Size number cast into top of handle AND in the logo on bottom
- Pattern number adjacent to logo
- Machine-polished, glassy-smooth cooking surface
- Thinner, lighter weight than modern cast iron
*If you see the word GRISWOLD anywhere on the bottom: stop and investigate fully before pricing.*

**ERIE (Pre-Griswold branding)** (1865–~1905)
Earliest pieces marked only "ERIE" — predates the Griswold name on pieces.
Spider legs on very early pieces (pre-1890s). Extremely rare. Any ERIE-marked piece: photograph and escalate.

**WAPAK** (Wapak, OH | 1903–1926)
"WAPAK" in raised letters, sometimes with an Indian head logo.
Less common than Griswold or Wagner. Legitimate collector interest. Values similar to Wagner.

**FAVORITE / PIQUA WARE** (Piqua, OH | ~1890s–1930s)
"FAVORITE" marking, sometimes "PIQUA WARE."
Strong collector base. Values 50–70% of comparable Griswold.
The "Smiley" skillet (distinctive pour spout design) is particularly sought.

### TIER 2 — MODERATE COLLECTOR VALUE

**WAGNER / WAGNER WARE** (Sidney, OH | 1891–1959)
Second most valuable after Griswold. Look for:
- "WAGNER" or "WAGNER WARE" in raised letters
- "SIDNEY -O-" foundry mark (Sidney, Ohio) on many pieces
- "QUALITY" sometimes appears
- Generally values 30–50% less than comparable Griswold
*Wagner acquired Griswold in 1959. Post-acquisition pieces marked both names are less collectible.*

**BSR / BIRMINGHAM STOVE & RANGE** (Birmingham, AL | 1900s–1990s)
"BSR" marking, sometimes "CENTURY" series.
Solid collector base especially in Southeast US.
Values 20–40% of comparable Griswold.

**LODGE** (South Pittsburg, TN | 1896–present)
Still in production — important distinction.
*Vintage Lodge* (pre-1960s, smooth cooking surface): moderate collector value.
*Modern Lodge* (bumpy texture, "LODGE" with country of origin): cookware value only, $20–$40 retail, not collectible.
Vintage Lodge with smooth surface: 30–50% of Griswold value.

### TIER 3 — COOKWARE VALUE ONLY (price as functional, not collectible)

- Modern Lodge (bumpy surface, post-1960s)
- Unmarked generic cast iron with no maker identification
- Chinese imports / reproductions (see Section 6)
- Camp Chef, Tramontina, other modern brands
- Any piece with "Made in China," "Taiwan," or similar

**Cookware-only floor pricing:**
Unmarked skillet, good condition: $8–$20
Modern Lodge skillet: $15–$30 (retail competition keeps ceiling low)
Generic dutch oven, good condition: $15–$35

## SECTION 3: GRISWOLD LOGO IDENTIFICATION & DATING

*This is the most important identification skill for Griswold. Logo alone determines value tier.*

Logo identification = flip the piece, look at the bottom mark. Match to table below.

---

### LOGO TIMELINE (chronological)

**ERIE LOGO** (~1865–1905)
- Marked only "ERIE" — no Griswold name yet
- Very early pieces have spider legs (heat ring legs allowing pan to sit in coals)
- EXTREME rarity — fewer than 200 known spider skillets exist
- Any ERIE-marked piece: DO NOT PRICE. Photograph and escalate to specialist.
- Estimated value: $200 to $10,000+ depending on specific piece

---

**GRISWOLD'S ERIE / SLANT LOGO** (~1905–1920)
- First logos to carry both "GRISWOLD" and "ERIE" together
- Letters in the logo are slanted/italic style
- With "ERIE PA USA": more common, earlier version
- Without "ERIE PA": later version (~1909–1920)
- These are the first truly "Griswold" pieces most collectors target
- Value: **add 50–100% premium over Large Block Logo prices**

---

**LARGE BLOCK LOGO (LBL)** (~1920–1940) ← THE MOST COMMON COLLECTIBLE
- "GRISWOLD" in large, upright block letters inside cross-and-circle
- "ERIE PA USA" below
- This is the logo most collectors mean when they say "Griswold"
- Most frequently found at estate sales
- The baseline for all Griswold pricing comparisons
- Two variants:
  - **Large EPU (Erie PA USA)**: most common, full city designation
  - **Small EPU**: less common, slightly different proportions

---

**SMALL BLOCK LOGO (SBL)** (~1939–1957)
- Same design as Large Block but noticeably smaller
- "ERIE" often absent from later examples
- Three handle variants during this period (handle design helps narrow date)
- Less valuable than Large Block — roughly 40–60% of LBL price
- Very late pieces (~1955–1957) made at Wagner foundry after acquisition: least collectible

---

**POST-ACQUISITION (1959–1973)**
- Wagner-made pieces using Griswold molds
- "Made in USA" often appears (Griswold originals rarely have this)
- Logo often appears on Wagner-pattern pieces — wrong proportions
- Dual logo (both Griswold and Wagner names): clearly post-acquisition
- NOT collectible in traditional sense. Price as vintage cookware only.
- Value: $20–$45 regardless of size

---

### GRISWOLD SIZE NUMBER SYSTEM

Griswold numbered by size, not by diameter. Key reference:

| Size # | Approx diameter | Notes |
|--------|----------------|-------|
| 2 | ~6" | RARE — very limited production |
| 3 | ~6.5" | Common |
| 4 | ~7" | Common |
| 5 | ~8" | Common |
| 6 | ~9" | Most common size |
| 7 | ~9.5" | Common |
| **8** | **~10.5"** | **Most common overall** |
| 9 | ~11" | Common |
| 10 | ~11.75" | Less common |
| 11 | ~12" | Less common |
| 12 | ~13.25" | Increasingly rare, valuable |
| 13 | ~14" | RARE — significant premium |
| 14 | ~15" | RARE — significant premium |
| 20 | ~20"+ | Hotel size — extremely rare |

**Rarity rule:** Common sizes are 3 through 9. Size 2 and sizes 12 through 20 command significant rarity premiums. A No. 13 or No. 14 in any logo era is worth investigating carefully.

## SECTION 4: WAGNER IDENTIFICATION

**Early Wagner** (~1891–1910s):
- Simple "WAGNER" in raised letters
- No city designation
- Earlier, rarer — moderate premium over later pieces

**Wagner Ware period** (~1920s–1950s):
- "WAGNER WARE" with "SIDNEY -O-" (Sidney, Ohio)
- Most commonly found Wagner at estate sales
- "QUALITY" sometimes appears alongside

**Post-Griswold acquisition** (1959 onward):
- May carry both Wagner and Griswold markings
- Less collectible, price as vintage cookware

**Wagner sizing:** Uses catalog number system rather than simple size numbers. A "1058" = regular skillet (105x), size #8. Less intuitive than Griswold but same sizes apply.

**Wagner value rule:** Take the comparable Griswold Large Block Logo price and multiply by 0.5–0.7 for equivalent Wagner piece in same condition.

## SECTION 5: PRICING BY CHANNEL

### Three channels you must understand:

**FLOOR** = Estate sale price. One weekend to sell. Motivated bargain buyer. Price for 70%+ sell-through.

**ONLINE (eBay)** = National buyer pool. Patient seller. 30–60 day timeline. 10–15% eBay fee. Subtract $8–$15 for shipping costs in buyer's mind.

**AUCTION** = Specialized collector auction (Dinky's, etc.). Competitive bidding. Hammer price + 15–25% buyer's premium. Represents peak collector demand. Only relevant for genuinely rare pieces.

**The channel decision rule:**
- Spread between floor and online < $30: sell on floor, not worth eBay effort
- Spread between floor and online > $50: strongly consider online listing
- Estimated value > $150: always consider online or auction
- Estimated value > $300: escalate to specialist or consign to auction

## SECTION 6: REPRODUCTIONS & FAKES — WHAT TO WATCH FOR

**The financial stakes:** Pricing a reproduction as authentic can expose you to client complaints, reputation damage, and potential liability. This section is non-optional reading.

### The Primary Fake Test (30 seconds)

**Texture test:** Run your palm across the cooking surface.
- Authentic vintage Griswold: smooth, almost glassy, machine-polished
- Fake/reproduction: rough, pebbly, bumpy texture — identical to modern Lodge
This single test catches the majority of crude fakes.

**Logo sharpness test:** Examine the lettering closely.
- Authentic: crisp, sharp letter edges, clean casting
- Fake: soft, fuzzy, or blurry letterforms — looks "melted"
- Fakes often have slightly wrong proportions or misspellings

**Weight test:** Authentic Griswold is lighter than you'd expect — thinner walls than modern cast iron.
- Unusually heavy piece for its size: red flag
- But: worn fakes can feel lighter — texture test is more reliable

---

### HIGHEST RISK ITEMS (most commonly faked)

**Griswold No. 0 / #562 Toy Skillet**
- Original: rare, sells $100–$175 authentic
- Fakes: crude casting, "Made in China" paper label often still present
- Tell: rough surface, sloppy lettering, wrong proportions
- Fake value: $5–$10. Real value: $100–$175. Stakes are real.

**Griswold #262/625 Corn Stick Pan**
- Original: "pointed tips of sticks extend completely to edge"
- Fake: pointed tips don't reach edge, pattern number often reads "282" or "252" instead of "262," sometimes "snick" instead of "stick"
- Very common fake — see every other estate sale

**Griswold ERIE #5 Skillet**
- One of the rarest legitimate pieces
- Recasts (made using a real pan as the mold) exist
- Recasts are slightly smaller than originals (metal shrinks when cooling from mold)
- Any ERIE #5: photograph and escalate, do not self-price

**Santa Cake Molds**
- Widely reproduced, fakes everywhere
- Escalate any Santa or figural mold before pricing

**"Griswold Pup" figurines**
- Predominantly reproductions on the market
- Of eBay listings searched in 2023 study: only 4 of 24 appeared authentic
- Do not price as collectible without expert verification

---

### RECASTS vs. FAKES — THE DISTINCTION MATTERS

**Fake:** Entirely new piece made to look like a collectible. Cheap Chinese imports are the most common. Easy to spot with texture test.

**Recast:** An actual vintage pan is used as a mold to cast a new piece. Much harder to detect because:
- The logo looks right (copied from real piece)
- The proportions look right
- The texture may even look right on high-quality recasts

**Recast detection:**
- Recasts are slightly smaller than originals (metal shrinks ~2% when cooling from mold)
- Logo detail slightly softer/less crisp than original
- If something looks right but "feels off," it may be a recast
- For any piece over $200 estimated value where authenticity is uncertain: escalate

---

### THE "MADE IN USA" TELL

Authentic Griswold pieces manufactured during the company's operation (pre-1957) almost never carry a "Made in USA" marking — country of origin labeling wasn't required for domestic goods at that time.

If you see "GRISWOLD" and "Made in USA" on the same piece: this is post-acquisition Wagner-made piece (1959–1973). Not the collectible Griswold. Price at vintage cookware tier ($20–$45).

## SECTION 7: CONDITION ASSESSMENT — THE DETAILED CHECKLIST

Run through this for any piece over $40 estimated value:

**Structural integrity**
☐ Cracks — hold to light, look across surface at angle, feel rim with finger
☐ Warping — place on flat hard surface, check for rocking or spinning
☐ Chips — check rim, handle tip, pour spouts
☐ Prior welds or repairs — look for discoloration lines, different surface texture in patches

**Surface condition**
☐ Rust level — light surface rust (orange) vs. deep pitting (cratered surface)
☐ Pitting depth — minor pitting reduces value 20–30%; heavy pitting reduces 50–70%
☐ Prior seasoning — irrelevant to value but reassures buyers who plan to cook with it
☐ Electroplating or paint — some pieces painted or chrome-plated, reduces collector value

**Logo/marking integrity**
☐ Logo legibility — crisp and clear vs. worn/faded (worn logo reduces value 15–25%)
☐ Size number visible on handle
☐ Pattern number visible
☐ All expected text present for the era

**Completeness**
☐ Lids present and matching (for dutch ovens, chicken fryers) — missing lid reduces value 30–50%
☐ Bail handle intact (for dutch ovens)
☐ Glass lid intact and unchipped (for Wagner square skillets)

## SECTION 8: THE CHANNEL DECISION — FLOOR vs. ONLINE vs. ESCALATE

### SELL ON FLOOR (price and tag, move on):
- Any piece under $60 estimated eBay value in good or fair condition
- Wagner pieces under No. 12 in good condition
- BSR/Lodge/generic pieces at any value
- Modern Lodge (price as $15–$30 cookware)
- Any cracked piece (price as $5–$15 cookware regardless of maker)

### LIST ONLINE (set aside for online sale, do not floor price):
- Any Griswold Large Block Logo in sizes 10, 11, 12 in excellent condition
- Any Griswold Slant Logo in any size, excellent condition
- Any piece with estimated eBay value over $80 where floor/online spread exceeds $40
- Complete matched sets (dutch oven + lid, chicken fryer + lid)
- Specialty pieces: waffle irons, unusual molds, gem pans in excellent condition
- Wagner sizes 12+ in excellent condition

### ESCALATE TO SPECIALIST (do not self-price):
- Any ERIE-marked piece (pre-Griswold branding)
- Any piece with spider legs
- Any Griswold No. 13, No. 14, No. 20
- Any piece where estimated value might exceed $200 and you're uncertain of authenticity
- Any Santa mold, figural piece, or highly unusual form
- Any piece a collector at your sale stops and stares at — trust the buyer's reaction

**Who to escalate to:**
- Dinky's Auction (Montgomery, IN) — the specialist cast iron auction house
- WGCA (Wagner and Griswold Collectors Association) — members often do free ID
- Griswold & Cast Iron Cookware Association (GCICA) — annual convention, active Facebook group
- CastIronCollector.com forums — post photo, get ID within hours from expert community

## SECTION 9: THE 5 MOST EXPENSIVE MISTAKES ESTATE SALE OPERATORS MAKE WITH CAST IRON

**Mistake 1: Selling an ERIE piece as "old Griswold"**
An ERIE-marked piece priced at $45 when it's worth $500–$5,000+. This is the nightmare scenario. Solution: any piece marked only "ERIE" or with spider legs — photograph and escalate, period.

**Mistake 2: Pricing a No. 13 or No. 14 as a No. 8**
Large skillets look like large skillets to the untrained eye. But a No. 13 Large Block Logo in excellent condition is worth $350–$600 online; a No. 8 is worth $85–$130. The size number is on the handle and the bottom. Read it.

**Mistake 3: Pricing a cracked piece at collector value**
A hairline crack on a Griswold No. 8 drops it from $85 to $10. Run your finger around the entire rim on every piece. Hold to light at an angle. Never skip this.

**Mistake 4: Assuming a smooth-looking piece is authentic Griswold**
High-quality recasts exist. For any piece where you'd price it over $150, spend 3 minutes on eBay sold listings confirming the specific logo variant and size match before tagging.

**Mistake 5: Pricing Wagner as Griswold**
Wagner is quality cast iron and legitimately collectible. But it's worth 50–70 cents on the dollar versus comparable Griswold. Check the bottom: does it say GRISWOLD or WAGNER WARE? They are different.

## SECTION 10: QUICK REFERENCE — IN THE FIELD

### 90-Second Identification Sequence
1. Flip over → read bottom marking
2. Run palm across cooking surface (smooth = vintage; rough = modern or fake)
3. Check for cracks (light + fingertip on rim)
4. Check for warping (set flat on surface)
5. Match logo to Section 3 or 4
6. Find size number on handle
7. Match to pricing table in Section 5
8. Apply condition multiplier
9. Make channel decision (Section 8)

### Immediate Escalation Triggers
- Says "ERIE" only (no Griswold name)
- Spider legs on bottom
- Size number 13, 14, or 20
- Any toy/miniature piece
- Any Santa or figural mold
- Buyer stops and stares at something you've overlooked

### Condition Killers (always check these)
- Cracks → price as cookware ($5–$15)
- Warping/spinning → reduce 40–60%
- Heavy pitting → reduce 50–70%
- "Made in USA" on Griswold → post-acquisition, price as vintage cookware ($20–$45)

### Best Resources for Field Verification
- **eBay Sold Listings**: search "[maker] [size #] [logo type] cast iron skillet" → filter Sold
- **CastIronCollector.com**: identification, marks, reproduction photos
- **GriswoldCookware.com**: auction results, pricing history, reproduction guides
- **GCICA Facebook Group**: photo ID by experts, usually within hours
- **WorthPoint** ($30/month): deep historical price archive

## SECTION 11: MARKET CONTEXT (Updated May 2026)

**The market has strengthened significantly since 2020.** A 2024 Dinky's Auction saw a Griswold ERIE spider skillet hit $10,000; a Griswold No. 1 pattern 411 hit $9,500. December 2024: Griswold No. 14 with front-pour spot sold $3,249 on eBay; No. 13 slant logo sold $2,799.

**Common pieces have also risen.** The blogger at GriswoldCookware.com documented: pieces that sold for $25 in 2010 now sell for $85–$150. The market appreciation has been consistent and sustained.

**Online has overtaken floor pricing.** The spread between estate sale floor and eBay has widened as collector awareness has grown. Buyers who attend estate sales increasingly know exactly what they're looking at. Price accordingly — do not underprice to move pieces fast when a 3-day eBay listing will return 2–3x more on quality pieces.

**Facebook collecting groups are active marketplaces.** The GCICA Facebook group and cast-iron-specific buy/sell groups move pieces at prices between floor and auction. If you're building an online presence, these groups are a direct-to-collector channel worth monitoring.

**Reproductions are increasing.** As prices rise, the incentive to fake increases. The volume of Chinese reproductions and domestic recasts appearing at estate sales has grown since 2020. This makes authentication discipline more important, not less.
