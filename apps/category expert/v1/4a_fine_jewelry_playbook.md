## THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT FACT IN FINE JEWELRY

Fine jewelry is the only estate sale category where a single overlooked item sitting in a bathroom drawer can be worth more than every other item in the entire house combined.

A 3-carat natural diamond solitaire ring in a cracked case: $15,000–$45,000.
A Tiffany & Co. platinum engagement ring in a tissue: $8,000–$50,000+.
An Art Deco platinum bracelet with sapphires: $5,000–$30,000.

**This category requires a non-negotiable rule: every piece gets individual attention. There is no such thing as a "jewelry box at $X each" pricing approach. Every single piece is examined before any piece is priced.**

The cost of applying this rule: 30–60 extra minutes per estate.
The cost of not applying it: pricing a $15,000 ring at $75.

---

## SECTION 1: THE TRIAGE QUESTION — WHAT IS THE METAL?

This is the first question for every piece, answered before any other assessment. The metal determines whether a piece has intrinsic commodity value that exists independent of design, condition, or desirability.

**SOLID PRECIOUS METAL** → Has melt value as absolute floor. Cannot be worth less than its metal content regardless of damage, style, or desirability.

**GOLD-FILLED / GOLD-PLATED / BASE METAL** → Has no intrinsic metal value. 100% decorative — see Costume Jewelry Playbook.

The mark on the piece tells you which you have. Always use a 10x loupe. Look inside rings, on necklace and bracelet clasps, on earring post backs, on pendant bales.

## SECTION 2: GOLD — THE COMPLETE MARK SYSTEM

### American Karat Marks

| Mark | Gold purity | Value per gram at $105/g gold* | Notes |
|------|------------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 24K or 999 | 99.9% | $104.90 | Pure gold — too soft for most jewelry; rare |
| 22K or 916 | 91.6% | $96.18 | Common in Indian, Middle Eastern jewelry |
| 18K or 750 | 75.0% | $78.75 | Luxury standard; deep color; European fine jewelry |
| 14K or 585 | 58.3% | $61.22 | Most common American jewelry gold |
| 10K or 417 | 41.7% | $43.79 | Minimum US legal gold; palest color; most durable |
| 9K or 375 | 37.5% | $39.38 | Common British vintage |

*Gold spot price as of May 2026 approximately $3,200–$3,400/troy oz = ~$103–$109/gram. Check Kitco.com every sale morning — this moves daily.*

### European Millesimal Fineness Numbers

Europeans stamp three-digit numbers representing parts per thousand of gold content. These appear constantly on imported pieces and are the most frequently missed marks at estate sales.

**750 = 18K gold. 585 = 14K gold. 417 = 10K gold. 375 = 9K gold.**

A piece with "750" stamped on the clasp and being sold in the costume tray is 18K gold worth its melt value. This happens constantly. Learn these numbers cold.

### The "Not Gold" Mark System — Complete List

If ANY of these appear without an additional karat/fineness mark: zero precious metal value.

| Mark | What it means | Value |
|------|--------------|-------|
| GP or GEP | Gold plated / electroplated | Decorative only |
| GF | Gold filled — 5% gold by weight, bonded | Decorative only |
| 1/20 12K GF | Gold filled notation | Decorative only |
| RGP or HGP | Rolled/heavy gold plated | Decorative only |
| HGE | Heavy gold electroplate | Decorative only |
| Gold Tone | Color only | Decorative only |
| No mark | Unknown — test before assuming | Assume decorative |

**Gold Filled nuance:** GF pieces from the 1940s–1960s are collectible vintage costume pieces in their own right — quality construction, durable finish — but they have no intrinsic metal value. Price as costume jewelry, not as gold.

### White Gold vs. Platinum

These look identical to the naked eye. The distinction is significant in value.

**White gold:** Gold alloyed with nickel, palladium, or zinc. Usually rhodium-plated on surface. Marked 14K WG, 18K, or simply 14K or 18K (white color implies white gold in context). Same melt calculation as yellow gold of same karat.

**Platinum:** Pure or near-pure platinum. Marked PT, PLAT, 950Pt, 900Pt, or 950 PLAT. Denser and heavier than gold of same size — a platinum ring feels noticeably heavier than a white gold ring of identical appearance. The mark is definitive.

**Critical dating rule:** Pre-WWII fine white jewelry (Art Deco 1920–1940, Edwardian 1900–1915) is almost always platinum — white gold wasn't commonly used until after WWII. Any geometric Art Deco ring in white metal with diamonds: assume platinum until the mark says otherwise.

Platinum spot price (May 2026): ~$1,000–$1,200/troy oz (~$32–$39/gram). Platinum is currently cheaper per gram than gold but the high density means platinum pieces are heavy and total melt value can be substantial.

## SECTION 3: THE GOLD MELT CALCULATION — YOUR ABSOLUTE PRICE FLOOR

Every piece of solid gold has a price floor defined by its metal content. This floor exists regardless of condition, style, damage, or desirability.

### The Formula

**Weight (grams) × Karat purity fraction × Current gold spot price per gram = Melt Value**

Karat purity fractions: 24K = 1.0, 22K = 0.916, 18K = 0.75, 14K = 0.583, 10K = 0.417, 9K = 0.375

**Gold spot price per gram = Gold spot price per troy oz ÷ 31.1**
At $3,300/troy oz: $3,300 ÷ 31.1 = $106.11 per gram

**Example:** 14K gold chain, 12 grams
12 × 0.583 × $106.11 = **$742 melt value**
Dealer will pay approximately 70–85% of melt: **$519–$631**

| 2g | $88 | $123 | $158 | $192 |

| 8g | $350 | $490 | $630 | $768 |

**Never price a gold piece below its melt value.** A damaged, ugly, unfashionable gold bracelet is still worth its melt value. Dealers who buy gold pay close to melt — your estate sale buyers often know this.

## SECTION 4: PLATINUM MELT CALCULATION

**Platinum melt value = Weight (grams) × 0.95 (for 950Pt) × Pt spot price per gram**

At $35/gram platinum: A 6-gram platinum ring × 0.95 × $35 = **$199 melt value**

Platinum pieces are heavy for their size. If an unmarked white metal piece feels significantly heavier than expected for its dimensions: strongly suspect platinum. Confirm with the mark or have a jeweler test it.

## SECTION 5: GEMSTONE IDENTIFICATION — THE ESCALATION FRAMEWORK

**Estate sale operators are not gemologists. The goal is not to grade stones — it is to know when to escalate.**

The most expensive mistake: pricing a genuine diamond ring as costume because the stone "looked fake." This can swing a piece from $75 to $15,000.

### The Escalation Rule — Non-Negotiable

**Any stone larger than 5mm in a gold or platinum setting: escalate to a jeweler before pricing.**

A 5mm round stone is approximately the size of a small pea — smaller than a typical engagement ring center stone. This threshold catches virtually all significant diamond and colored stone risks.

**Cost of escalation:** 10 minutes at a local jeweler, free to $25.
**Cost of missing it:** Catastrophic.

### Diamond Field Screening Tests

These are screening tools only. Any piece that passes these in gold/platinum: escalate.

**The fog test (most reliable home test):**
Breathe on the stone like fogging a mirror. A genuine diamond disperses heat almost instantaneously — fog clears in 1–2 seconds. CZ (cubic zirconia) holds fog for 4+ seconds. Glass holds even longer.

**The loupe test (10x magnification):**
- Natural diamonds almost always have inclusions — tiny carbon specks, feathers, clouds visible under magnification
- A completely flawless stone under magnification is suspicious — CZ is manufactured to perfection
- Diamond facet edges are razor sharp; CZ facets have slightly rounded edges
- Moissanite: look for doubling of facet lines when viewed through the table (top) of the stone — moissanite is doubly refractive; you'll see two of each facet line

**The setting test:**
Expensive stones are set in expensive metals. A diamond-like stone in a 14K or platinum setting is almost certainly not CZ — jewelers don't set cubic zirconia in 18K platinum. If the setting is real gold: the stone warrants closer scrutiny.

**The marking test:**
If the setting is marked CZ: it's cubic zirconia. If the setting is unmarked or marked with gold/platinum: look more carefully.

### What Cannot Be Determined in the Field

- Natural diamond vs. lab-grown diamond (requires spectroscopy — lab diamonds are chemically and optically identical to natural; lab 1-carat ≈ $500–$1,500; natural 1-carat ≈ $3,000–$8,000+)
- Natural ruby/sapphire/emerald vs. synthetic vs. glass (requires gemologist)
- Pearl quality: natural vs. cultured vs. imitation (requires specialist)
- Diamond color and clarity grades (requires professional grading under standardized light)
- Any stone requiring a GIA/IGI grading report for accurate valuation

### Diamond Rough Valuation Context
*For escalation decision-making only — not for pricing*

At the wholesale/secondary market level (what estate sale buyers actually pay):
- 0.5 carat round, G color, VS2 clarity: $1,200–$1,800
- 1.0 carat round, G color, VS2 clarity: $3,500–$5,500
- 1.5 carat round, G color, VS2 clarity: $7,000–$11,000
- 2.0 carat round, G color, VS2 clarity: $13,000–$20,000
- 3.0+ carat: exponential premium; escalate to auction house

These are retail estimates. Secondary market (estate sale → buyer resale) typically 40–60% of retail. But these figures illustrate why the escalation rule is non-negotiable.

**Old European Cut and Old Mine Cut diamonds** (pre-1920 cutting styles, found in Victorian and Edwardian jewelry): Different from modern brilliant cuts — higher crown, smaller table, larger culet. These antique cuts command premiums among collectors. Do not discount them as "inferior cuts."

### Colored Stones — Escalation Matrix

| Stone | In gold/platinum setting | In base metal setting |
|-------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| Ruby (red corundum) | ESCALATE | Research; likely synthetic/glass |
| Sapphire (blue or any color) | ESCALATE | Research; likely synthetic |
| Emerald (green beryl) | ESCALATE | Research; likely synthetic/glass |
| Diamond | ESCALATE if >5mm | Unlikely real if base metal |
| Alexandrite (color-changing) | ESCALATE immediately | Likely synthetic |
| Opal | Research; Australian black opal very valuable | Likely imitation |
| Turquoise | Research Southwest pieces | Usually genuine if signed |
| Amethyst, garnet, topaz | Price at metal melt + modest premium | Costume pricing |

## SECTION 6: PEARLS — THE THREE-TIER SYSTEM

**Tooth test:** Gently rub pearl against front teeth. Real pearls (natural and cultured) feel slightly gritty. Fake/imitation pearls feel perfectly smooth like plastic.

| Type | Description | Value |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Natural | Formed entirely without human intervention — extremely rare | $5,000–$500,000+ per strand |
| Cultured | Human-implanted nucleus; real nacre; majority of "real" pearls | $200–$5,000 per strand |
| Imitation | Glass or plastic coated with pearlescent finish — smooth to teeth test | $2–$15 per strand |

**Mikimoto exception:** Any piece marked MIKIMOTO: escalate immediately. Genuine Mikimoto cultured pearl strands: $500–$8,000+.

## SECTION 7: FINE JEWELRY ERAS AND THEIR SIGNATURES

Period identification adds collector premium above melt value for pieces with distinctive period characteristics:

**Georgian (1714–1837):** Handmade, closed-back settings, foil-backed stones, mourning jewelry with hair. Extremely rare to find genuine Georgian. Any gold jewelry with closed-back settings: investigate.

**Victorian (1837–1901):** Ornate, nature-inspired motifs, yellow gold, pearls, garnets, seed pearls. Mourning jewelry (jet, black glass, hair) is a specific subcategory. Mid-value range.

**Edwardian (1901–1915):** Platinum or platinum-topped gold, intricate milgrain, filigree, diamonds, sapphires. Lacy, delicate aesthetic. Pre-WWII white metal + diamonds = assume platinum.

**Art Nouveau (1890–1910):** Flowing organic forms, enamel (plique-à-jour), female figures with flowing hair, dragonflies, orchids. Signed pieces by René Lalique, Alphonse Mucha, Fouquet: escalate immediately.

**Art Deco (1920–1940):** Geometric, bold, platinum and white gold, black enamel, calibré-cut colored stones, step cuts. This era produces many high-value finds at estate sales.

**Retro (1935–1950):** Large, bold yellow gold pieces, rose gold, amethyst, aquamarine, synthetic rubies. Less refined than Deco but gaining collector interest.

**Mid-Century Modern (1950–1970):** Clean lines, abstract forms, yellow gold, cultured pearls, cocktail rings with large colored stones.

## SECTION 9: CONDITION ASSESSMENT

### Condition Affects Collector Premium — Not Melt Floor

For gold: a badly damaged piece is still worth its melt value. Condition affects only the premium above melt.

**Issues that reduce collector premium (not melt value):**
- Prong damage or missing prongs: reduces 20–40%; stones may fall out
- Missing stones: reduces value; disclose
- Heavy scratching or worn surface: reduces 15–30%
- Solder repairs or alterations: reduces 20–50%
- Poor sizing or ring shank work: reduces 10–25%
- Monograms or engraving: reduces collector value 10–20%; no melt impact

**Issues that reduce melt value:**
- None — melt is melt

**Documentation that increases value:**
- Original GIA/IGI/AGS grading report for diamonds: significant premium
- Original box (especially Tiffany blue box): 15–30% premium
- Original receipt or provenance: modest to significant premium
- Appraisal documentation: helpful for pricing but not the same as grading report

## SECTION 10: PRICING FRAMEWORK

### The Three-Number Approach

Every fine jewelry piece requires three numbers:
1. **Melt value** — the floor, calculated from weight and current spot price
2. **Collector market value** — based on maker, era, design, and gemstones
3. **Your estate sale floor price** — at or above melt, below or at market depending on channel decision

## SECTION 11: THE 5 MOST EXPENSIVE MISTAKES

**Mistake 1: Missing European fineness marks**
A bracelet marked 750 in the costume jewelry tray. That's 18K gold. At 10 grams: ~$788 melt value. Learn 750, 585, 375 as well as you know 14K and 18K.

**Mistake 2: Pricing a large diamond as CZ**
An engagement ring with a 1.5-carat Old European Cut diamond priced at $95 as "probably costume." Actual value: $7,000–$12,000. The gold setting and the age of the cut are the tells. If it's in gold and it's bigger than a pea: escalate.

**Mistake 3: Pricing Tiffany at melt**
A Tiffany & Co. platinum diamond engagement ring priced at the melt value of the metal (~$300). The Tiffany name adds 3–10x the melt value. Any T&CO or TIFFANY & CO. mark: escalate before any pricing.

**Mistake 4: Not weighing gold pieces**
An unsigned 14K gold chain priced at $45 because it "looked like a simple piece." It weighs 18 grams. Melt value: $1,100. Price below melt means your client lost $1,000.

**Mistake 5: Treating GF as gold**
A bracelet marked 1/20 12K GF priced at $250 based on weight calculation. Gold-filled contains 5% gold by weight. Actual precious metal value: approximately $12. The GF mark is the tell. Read the clasp.

## SECTION 12: QUICK REFERENCE — IN THE FIELD

### 2-Minute Triage Sequence
1. Find the mark — use loupe
2. Does it say 10K/417, 14K/585, 18K/750, 22K/916, PT/PLAT, 925? → Real precious metal
3. Does it say GF, GP, RGP, no mark? → Costume jewelry playbook
4. Weigh it → calculate melt
5. Any stone >5mm in gold or platinum? → Escalate
6. Designer mark? → Check escalation list
7. Price at melt + appropriate premium, or escalate

### Gold Mark Quick Reference
- 24K / 999 = pure gold
- 18K / 750 = luxury gold, most valuable common alloy
- 14K / 585 = standard American gold, most common
- 10K / 417 = minimum US legal gold
- 9K / 375 = British vintage gold
- GF / GP / RGP = NOT GOLD
