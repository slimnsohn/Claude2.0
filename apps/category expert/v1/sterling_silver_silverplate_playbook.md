## THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT FACT IN THIS CATEGORY

**Sterling silver and silverplate are not the same thing and must never be priced the same way.**

Sterling silver has intrinsic metal value — real silver, real money, tracked daily on commodity exchanges. A pile of sterling flatware is worth a calculable dollar amount regardless of condition, pattern, or desirability. That floor never disappears.

Silverplate has no meaningful intrinsic metal value. The silver layer is microscopically thin — electroplated onto base metal. A silverplate dealer will not buy it for melt. Its entire value is decorative and collector-driven, and that value is modest for most pieces.

The difference between a sterling flatware set and a silverplate flatware set at an estate sale can be $3,000 vs. $45. They can look identical to the untrained eye. The marks on the back of the handle tell you which you have. This is the skill that matters most in this category.

## SECTION 1: THE FUNDAMENTAL DISTINCTION — STERLING vs. SILVERPLATE

### What Sterling Silver IS

Sterling silver is an alloy of 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% copper (or other hardening metals). The copper is added because pure silver is too soft for practical use. Every piece of genuine sterling has a definable, calculable metal value based on its weight and the current silver spot price.

Sterling is always marked. American law and international convention require it. The marks may be worn, small, or in unexpected places — but they are there. A piece with no marks at all that is claimed to be sterling requires physical testing to verify.

### What Silverplate IS

Silverplate is base metal — usually copper, nickel silver (which contains NO actual silver), or brass — with a microscopically thin layer of silver deposited on the surface through electrolysis. The silver layer is typically 0.0001 to 0.0003 inches thick. It looks like silver, polishes like silver, tarnishes like silver. It is not silver in any meaningful quantity.

A weighted sterling candlestick experiment documented at Redollar.com found only 17.22 grams of actual sterling silver in a piece whose total weight suggested much more — the remaining 90% of the weight was plaster and cement filler. This is the trap: weight is not a reliable indicator of silver content.

### The One Test That Settles It

**Look at the marks on the back of the handle or base.**

If it says **STERLING** or **925** or **.925**: it is sterling silver. Stop. Calculate melt value. Assess collector premium.

If it says **EPNS, EP, A1, Triple Plate, Quadruple Plate, IS, Silverplate, Silver Plated, Deep Silver, Inlaid**: it is silverplate. Value is decorative only.

If it says **1847 Rogers Bros** without the word STERLING: it is silverplate. The 1847 Rogers Bros line was exclusively silverplated.

If it says **WM Rogers, Oneida Community Plate, Holmes & Edwards** without STERLING: silverplate.

There is no gray area here. The word STERLING or the number 925 must appear. Everything else is plate.

## SECTION 2: MARK IDENTIFICATION SYSTEM — COMPLETE FIELD REFERENCE

### American Sterling Marks (most common at US estate sales)

The United States uses a word-based system rather than pictorial hallmarks. American sterling identification is straightforward:

**STERLING** — the word itself, stamped on the piece. The primary American mark since the late 19th century. If you see this word: sterling confirmed.

**925** or **.925** or **925/1000** — the numeric purity mark. International standard. Means 92.5% silver. If you see this: sterling confirmed.

**COIN** or **COIN SILVER** — older American mark, pre-Civil War era primarily. Indicates 90% silver (slightly lower than sterling). Genuine silver, calculable melt value. Often found on 18th and early 19th century American pieces by named silversmiths. Can be highly valuable.

**GORHAM marks**: Lion + Anchor + Capital G (the famous Gorham triad). Always accompanied by STERLING on genuine Gorham sterling pieces. The lion and anchor alone without STERLING: do not assume sterling — Gorham also made silverplate.

**TIFFANY & CO.**: If it says Tiffany & Co. and STERLING: escalate immediately. If it says Tiffany & Co. without STERLING: still potentially valuable but confirm.

**REED & BARTON**: Look for the name plus STERLING. Reed & Barton made both sterling and silverplate — the mark alone does not confirm sterling.

---

### British Sterling Marks (frequently found at estate sales — know these)

Britain has the oldest and most comprehensive hallmarking system in the world, dating to 1300. British silver is extremely common at American estate sales because of import history. A complete British hallmark contains up to five symbols:

**LION PASSANT** (walking lion, facing left) — The primary British sterling purity mark. Has been used since 1544. If you see a walking lion on silver: British sterling confirmed. This is the equivalent of the American STERLING stamp.

**MAKER'S MARK** — Initials of the silversmith or company, usually in a shaped cartouche (shield, oval, rectangle). Appears first in the sequence of marks.

**ASSAY OFFICE MARK** — Symbol representing the city where the piece was tested and hallmarked:
- Leopard's head (crowned pre-1821, uncrowned after): **London**
- Anchor: **Birmingham**  
- Crown: **Sheffield**
- Castle: **Edinburgh** (Scotland)
- Harp crowned: **Dublin** (Ireland)

**DATE LETTER** — A letter in a shaped cartouche indicating the year of manufacture. The font, case (upper/lower), and cartouche shape all vary by assay office and cycle. British silver can be dated to the exact year using date letter tables. Resource: 925-1000.com has complete date letter tables for all British assay offices.

**DUTY MARK** (monarch's head, 1784–1890) — Profile of the reigning monarch. Indicates duty was paid. Useful for narrowing date range.

**Practical British silver field rule:** If you see a walking lion anywhere on a piece alongside other small marks: it is British sterling. Confirm with Maker's mark and assay office mark for further identification.

---

### Continental European Marks (increasingly common at estate sales)

**FRENCH SILVER:**
- **Minerva head** (profile of helmeted woman): 950/1000 silver (95% pure — higher than sterling). First standard. Imported piece mark: owl.
- French silver is often marked with Minerva + maker's mark + sometimes a guarantee mark (eagle head for 18-carat gold, but Minerva specifically for silver).
- 950 silver = higher purity than American sterling. Calculate melt value at 0.950, not 0.925.

**GERMAN SILVER:**
- **Crescent + Crown**: German standard mark for 800/1000 silver (80% pure). IMPORTANT: 800 silver is real silver — just lower purity than sterling. Still has significant melt value, just calculate at 0.800.
- Also look for **800** stamped directly on the piece.
- German silver is very common at estate sales — especially hollowware, tea services, serving pieces.
- Do not confuse "German silver" (a term for nickel silver alloy with NO actual silver) with German-made silver marked with the crescent and crown.

**SCANDINAVIAN SILVER:**
- Norway, Sweden, Denmark: often marked with **830S** (83% silver) or **925S**
- Georg Jensen (Danish): marked GJ or Georg Jensen — among the most valuable silver in the world. If you see Georg Jensen marks: escalate immediately.

**RUSSIAN SILVER:**
- Pre-revolutionary Russian silver: **Kokoshnik mark** (woman's profile in traditional headdress) + purity number (84 = approximately 875/1000 silver, 88 = 916/1000)
- Russian silver is frequently underpriced at estate sales. Kokoshnik mark = genuine Russian imperial silver.

**ITALIAN SILVER:**
- Often marked **800** directly
- Sometimes marked with regional marks + 800

**800 SILVER RULE:** Any piece marked 800 is real silver, 80% pure. Calculate melt value at weight × 0.800 × spot price. Do not dismiss it as "not sterling" — it has substantial melt value.

### The Weighted Sterling Problem

A special category that deceives operators constantly: **STERLING WEIGHTED** or **WEIGHTED STERLING** or **REINFORCED**.

These pieces ARE sterling silver — but only on the outside. Inside they are filled with plaster, cement, sand, or gypsum to add weight and stability. Common weighted items:
- Candlesticks (the most common weighted sterling item)
- Salt and pepper shakers
- Compotes
- Some hollow-handled serving pieces

**The weighted sterling trap:** A pair of sterling weighted candlesticks might weigh 2 pounds total. You calculate the melt value on 2 pounds of sterling: substantial money. But only about 10% of that weight is actual silver — the rest is filler. A documented experiment found only 17.22 grams of real silver in a standard weighted candlestick pair. At current silver prices (~$32/oz in May 2026), that's roughly $18 in silver content for a piece that weighs 400 grams total.

**Weighted sterling melt value reference** (at $32/oz silver spot price, May 2026):
- Standard pair of weighted sterling candlesticks: $15–$35 in silver content
- Weighted sterling salt and pepper set: $8–$15 in silver content
- Weighted sterling compote: $20–$45 in silver content

**Weighted sterling collector value:** Minimal. Noted silversmiths and prestigious manufacturers generally avoided weighted production — it was considered a compromise. Most weighted sterling is priced on decorative value ($15–$45 per candlestick pair) unless the maker or pattern is unusually desirable.

**How to identify weighted sterling in the field:**
- Marked "STERLING WEIGHTED," "WEIGHTED," or "REINFORCED" — these terms are legally required to appear
- Candlesticks: tap the base — hollow sound indicates weighted fill
- Base is often sealed with felt or baize covering the bottom — lift it if possible to check for fill seams
- Heavy for its size but marked sterling — probably weighted

## SECTION 3: THE MELT VALUE CALCULATION — YOUR PRICE FLOOR

Every piece of sterling silver has a price floor defined by its metal content. This floor exists regardless of condition, pattern, damage, or desirability. Even a badly dented, monogrammed, unfashionable sterling piece is worth its melt value.

### The Formula

**Melt Value = Weight (troy oz) × 0.925 × Current Silver Spot Price**

Sterling is 92.5% silver, so you multiply by 0.925 to get the actual silver content.

**Unit conversion:** Most kitchen scales weigh in grams. 1 troy ounce = 31.1 grams.
So: Weight in grams ÷ 31.1 × 0.925 × spot price = melt value

**Current silver spot price:** Check Kitco.com or APMEX.com for the live price. Silver fluctuates daily. As of May 2026, silver has been trading in the $30–$35/troy oz range. Do not use outdated prices — check the day of the sale.

### The Dealer Discount

Silver buyers and dealers pay approximately 70–90% of calculated melt value. They need a margin for their processing, testing, and refining costs. This means:
- If melt value calculates to $100: expect to receive $70–$90 from a dealer
- Your estate sale floor price should reflect this: price sterling at 80–100% of melt value to attract buyers who know they're getting fair value
- Rare patterns and prestigious makers: price ABOVE melt value (see Section 5)

### Melt Value Quick Reference

*At $32/troy oz silver spot price (verify current price — this changes daily)*

| Weight | Sterling melt value | You receive (~85%) |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 1 oz (28g) | $29.60 | ~$25 |
| 2 oz (57g) | $59.20 | ~$50 |
| 4 oz (113g) | $118.40 | ~$100 |
| 8 oz (227g) | $236.80 | ~$200 |
| 1 lb (454g) | $473.60 | ~$400 |
| 2 lb (908g) | $947.20 | ~$800 |

**Practical field tool:** Download the APMEX Sterling Silver Scrap Calculator app or bookmark silverrecyclers.com/Calculators/ster_calculator.aspx. Weigh pieces on a kitchen scale, enter the weight, get the melt value instantly. Do this for every piece of confirmed sterling before pricing.

### Knife Exception

Sterling flatware knife handles are often hollow sterling with stainless steel blades. Only the handle contains silver. Estimate the handle at approximately 30–40% of the total knife weight for melt calculation purposes.

## SECTION 5: FLATWARE — THE MOST COMMON STERLING FIND

Flatware sets are the most frequently encountered sterling at estate sales. The evaluation framework:

### Step 1: Confirm Sterling vs. Plate (Section 2)
Non-negotiable first step. Check the back of every handle.

### Step 2: Identify the Maker
Read the full marking on the back of the handle. Most American flatware clearly names the maker.

### Step 3: Identify the Pattern
Many pieces have the pattern name stamped near the maker's mark. If not, use Replacements.com — photograph the handle design and search by visual description. It identifies patterns faster than any other resource.

### Step 4: Calculate Melt Value
Weigh the full set. Calculate as described in Section 3. This is your absolute price floor.

### Step 5: Assess Collector Premium
Is this a top-tier pattern? (Tiffany Chrysanthemum, Jensen Acorn, Francis I, Grand Baroque, Chantilly, Strasbourg, Buttercup, Grand Baroque) — price significantly above melt.
Is this a mid-tier pattern? — price 20–50% above melt for clean complete sets.
Is this an undistinguished pattern from a volume maker? — price at melt or 10% above.

### Step 6: Count and Assess Completeness
A complete service for 8 or 12 with serving pieces commands a premium per piece vs. an incomplete set.
Standard 5-piece place setting: dinner fork, salad fork, dinner knife, teaspoon, soup spoon.
Serving pieces: cold meat fork, gravy ladle, tablespoon, butter knife, sugar spoon, salad serving fork and spoon. These often sell at 2–3x the individual place setting piece rate.

### Step 7: Check for Monograms
Monograms on collector pieces: reduce value 10–30% (buyers generally prefer non-monogrammed for pattern matching).
Monograms on melt-value pieces: irrelevant — melt value is the same.

### Step 8: Condition Assessment for Flatware
- Bowl of spoons: look for deep scratches, dents, bent tines on forks, worn pattern
- Heavy utensil wear on spoon bowls: reduces value 15–25%
- Bent tines: reduces value 30–50% per piece (can sometimes be straightened, but assume not)
- Repairs, soldering, cracks: significant reduction

## SECTION 6: HOLLOWWARE — TEA SERVICES, TRAYS, CANDLESTICKS, BOWLS

Hollowware is the high-ceiling, high-variance category. A sterling Gorham tea service can be worth $5,000–$20,000. A silverplate tea service of identical appearance: $45–$150.

### Tea and Coffee Services

A full sterling silver tea/coffee service typically includes: coffee pot, teapot, creamer, sugar bowl, waste bowl, and tray. The tray alone is often the most valuable piece by weight.

**Confirm EVERY piece:** In mixed estates, it's common to find a sterling tea service where the tray is silverplate and the pots are sterling, or vice versa. Check the mark on each individual piece.

**Gorham sterling tea service (5–7 pieces):** $5,000–$20,000 depending on pattern, condition, and period
**Tiffany sterling tea service:** $15,000–$50,000+
**Average quality American sterling tea service:** $800–$3,000 (calculate melt on all pieces, add 30–80% for maker and condition)
**British sterling tea service:** Calculate melt value, then research maker and period. Georgian-era British silver (pre-1840): significant additional premium.

### Trays and Salvers

Sterling trays are priced primarily on weight because the silver content is substantial. A large sterling tray can weigh 40–80 oz troy — at $32/oz spot, that's $1,184–$2,368 in silver content alone.

**The tray calculation:** Weigh the tray. Calculate melt value. Add 20–50% for Tier 1 makers, 10–20% for quality makers.

Watch for silverplate trays with sterling pieces — this happens. Check the mark on the tray independently.

### Candlesticks

Critical distinction:
- **STERLING WEIGHTED candlesticks:** See Section 2. Only ~10% is silver. Value: $30–$80 per pair at estate sale floor. Do not weigh and calculate full melt value.
- **SOLID STERLING candlesticks:** Fully solid silver, no fill. Calculate full melt value. Much less common. Identify by: no "WEIGHTED" mark, heavier uniform feel, tapping produces a denser sound. Seek these out.
- **SILVERPLATE candlesticks:** Decorative value only. $15–$45 per pair.

### Bowls, Pitchers, Vases

Solid sterling hollowware: calculate melt value by weight, add collector premium for notable makers.
Weighted sterling hollowware: deduct estimated filler weight (30–60% of total weight) before calculating melt.
Silverplate hollowware: decorative value $10–$60 depending on condition and maker.

## SECTION 7: SILVERPLATE — WHAT IT'S ACTUALLY WORTH

Silverplate is not worthless — but its value is entirely decorative and collector-driven, and that value is modest for most pieces. It has no melt value for practical purposes. Precious metal dealers will not buy it.

### Silverplate Value Framework

**Condition is paramount for silverplate** in a way it is not for sterling. Sterling retains melt value regardless of condition. Silverplate with worn or missing plating (showing copper or brass beneath) has minimal decorative value — it can't be repaired economically.

**Worn/re-plated appearance:** $3–$12 regardless of maker
**Good plating, no visible wear-through:** baseline silverplate pricing
**Excellent plating, no wear, complete set:** maximum silverplate pricing

### Silverplate Pricing by Category

**Flatware sets:**
- 1847 Rogers Bros, Oneida Community Plate, Holmes & Edwards in excellent condition, complete service 8: $30–$80 as a set
- Individual pieces: $1–$5 each
- Partial sets: difficult to sell — buyers want complete sets for pattern matching
- Exception: Community Plate patterns (Morning Star, Lasting Rose, White Orchid) have collector followings: $60–$150 for a complete service

**Tea services:**
- Silverplate tea service in excellent condition, well-made: $45–$150 as a service
- Individual pieces (creamer, sugar): $8–$25 each
- Damaged, worn plating: $5–$20 as a service for decorator use

**Serving pieces:**
- Silverplate serving bowl, good condition: $8–$25
- Silverplate tray, good condition: $15–$45
- Silverplate candlesticks, good condition: $15–$40 per pair

**Novelty and specialty silverplate:**
- Some early Sheffield plate (pre-1840 fused silver on copper, not electroplate) has collector value: $50–$300+ for quality pieces. Identify by: often no marks, look for copper showing through at edges (Sheffield plate shows copper at worn edges while electroplate shows yellow base metal)

### Silverplate Makers With Collector Premium

Not all silverplate is created equal. These makers command modest premiums:
- **Christofle** (French): higher quality silverplate, collector following. $50–$200 for serving pieces.
- **WMF** (German, Württembergische Metallwarenfabrik): Art Nouveau silverplate, strong collector market. $40–$200+ for serving pieces. Often marked WMF with an ostrich mark.
- **Elkington & Co.** (British, inventors of electroplating): early Elkington pieces have historical significance. $30–$150.
- **Rogers Bros. early triple plate**: modest collector premium, $15–$40 per piece in excellent condition.

## SECTION 8: COIN SILVER — THE OVERLOOKED CATEGORY

Coin silver is a pre-Civil War American silver standard: 90% silver (vs. 92.5% sterling). Marked **COIN** or **COIN SILVER** on older pieces.

**Why this matters at estate sales:** Coin silver pieces are frequently overlooked because they predate the STERLING mark era. A piece marked only COIN by a named Philadelphia, New York, or Boston silversmith can be extremely valuable as an antique — not just for melt value but for historical significance and maker premium.

**Key identifiers:**
- Marked COIN or COIN SILVER
- Often has maker's initials or full name in a cartouche
- Simple, Federal or Empire style forms typical
- Heavier, thicker construction than later machine-made pieces
- Fiddle-back handle shape common on early flatware

**Value:** Named American colonial or early Federal silversmiths command extraordinary premiums. Any piece marked with a named silversmith and COIN: photograph and research the maker before pricing. Paul Revere silver, for example, has sold for tens of thousands per piece at auction.

**Melt calculation for coin silver:** Weight × 0.900 × spot price (not 0.925 — coin silver is 90%, not 92.5%)

## SECTION 9: CONDITION ASSESSMENT

### For Sterling (melt value always exists regardless of condition)

**Condition affects collector premium, not melt floor:**
- Bent, dented, or damaged sterling: price at melt value only
- Repairs, soldering, patches: price at melt value only, disclose to buyers
- Monograms: deduct 10–30% from collector price; no impact on melt
- Tarnish: normal, expected, fully reversible — no value impact
- Polish wear (pattern worn away): reduces collector premium 20–40%; no impact on melt

**For hollowware, check specifically:**
- Dents and dings: reduce collector value 20–40%; melt value unaffected
- Repaired cracks or splits: significant reduction in collector value; disclose
- Missing components (lids, handles): reduce value 30–60%
- Engraving or crests: modest impact on collector value; no impact on melt

### For Silverplate (no melt floor — condition is everything)

| Condition | Description | Value impact |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Excellent | Full plating, no wear-through, all original finish | Full price |
| Good | Minor plating wear, no significant copper showing | 60–80% of excellent price |
| Fair | Visible plating wear, some copper/brass showing | 20–40% of excellent price |
| Poor | Heavy wear-through, significant base metal visible | Decorator/display only: 10–20% |
| Damaged | Bent, dented, broken, or heavily pitted | Minimal: $2–$8 |

## SECTION 11: THE 6 MOST EXPENSIVE MISTAKES IN THIS CATEGORY

**Mistake 1: Treating silverplate as sterling**
A set of 1847 Rogers Bros flatware priced at $400 (sterling equivalent pricing). Actual value: $40–$65 as a silverplate service in good condition. The marks are on the back of every handle. Read them.

**Mistake 2: Weighing weighted sterling and calculating full melt value**
A pair of weighted sterling candlesticks weighs 400 grams. Full melt calculation at $32/oz: ~$375. Actual silver content: ~17 grams = ~$17. You've priced based on the filler weight, not the silver content. Always look for the word WEIGHTED on the base.

**Mistake 3: Missing 800-grade European silver**
A German serving piece marked with a crescent and crown, priced at $15 as "silverplate" because it's not marked STERLING. It's 800/1000 silver — genuine silver with real melt value. Calculate melt at weight × 0.800 × spot price.

**Mistake 4: Pricing a top-tier pattern at melt value**
A complete Gorham Chantilly service for 12 priced at melt value (~$800). Collector value: $3,000–$8,000. The pattern name is stamped on the back of the handle. Five minutes on Replacements.com would have identified it. Use Replacements.com to identify every flatware pattern before pricing.

**Mistake 5: Selling a sterling tea service piecemeal**
Individual pieces from a sterling tea service priced separately at $40–$80 each. The complete service as a set: $1,200–$3,000. Always sell complete sets together. Never break up a service unless pieces are definitively from different patterns.

**Mistake 6: Missing coin silver**
An early American piece marked COIN by a named silversmith priced at melt value ($35). A piece by a named Philadelphia silversmith of the Federal period could be worth $300–$3,000+ as a historical artifact. Any COIN mark with a named maker: research before pricing.

## SECTION 12: QUICK REFERENCE — IN THE FIELD

### 90-Second Identification Sequence
1. Find the mark (back of handle, underside of base)
2. Does it say STERLING or 925? → Sterling: calculate melt value
3. Does it say EPNS, EP, A1, IS, Rogers Bros, Community Plate? → Silverplate: decorative value only
4. Does it say WEIGHTED or REINFORCED? → Sterling but only ~10% is silver: price as decorative
5. Do you see a walking lion? → British sterling: confirmed
6. Does it say 800 or show crescent+crown? → European 800 silver: calculate melt at 0.800
7. Does it say COIN? → 90% silver, possible historical value: research maker
8. Does it say Georg Jensen, Tiffany, or Gorham Martelé? → Escalate

### The Four Marks That Confirm Sterling
- The word **STERLING** (American)
- The number **925** or **.925** (international)
- The **Lion Passant** (walking lion, British)
- The **Minerva head** (French 950)

### The Marks That Mean Silverplate
- EPNS / EP / A1 / XII
- 1847 Rogers Bros / WM Rogers / IS alone
- Triple Plate / Quadruple Plate
- The word "Plate" or "Plated" anywhere

### Essential Tools for This Category
- **Digital kitchen scale** — weigh everything sterling before pricing
- **Loupe (10x magnification)** — marks are small; squinting doesn't work
- **Kitco.com or APMEX app** — live silver spot price
- **Silverrecyclers.com/Calculators** — sterling melt value calculator
- **Replacements.com** — flatware pattern identification
- **925-1000.com** — British hallmark date letter tables

## SECTION 13: MARKET CONTEXT (Updated May 2026)

**Silver prices are near multi-year highs.** At $30–$35/troy oz (versus $15–$20 in 2019–2020), the melt floor for sterling has roughly doubled in five years. This makes correctly identifying sterling vs. plate more financially critical than ever — the gap between the two has widened significantly.

**Pattern-matching collector market remains strong.** Replacements.com reports steady demand for Chantilly, Francis I, and Grand Baroque as collectors and pattern-completers seek pieces. Complete services in excellent condition continue to sell above melt by substantial margins.

**Georg Jensen has appreciated dramatically.** Jensen prices have more than doubled in the past decade. Any Jensen piece at an estate sale is a top-priority find.

**Silverplate market is soft.** The decorative silverplate market has weakened as younger buyers show less interest in formal entertaining pieces. Silverplate tea services and serving pieces that sold for $150–$300 in 2010 may sell for $45–$100 today. Adjust pricing expectations downward for silverplate relative to older reference guides.

**The "1847 Rogers Bros" confusion never goes away.** It is the single most frequently misidentified silver item at estate sales. The family name Rogers + the date 1847 in the brand name leads sellers to believe they have old, valuable sterling. It is silverplate. Every time. Read the marks.

**Weighted sterling candlesticks are everywhere.** They were an enormously popular wedding gift from the 1940s through the 1970s. Every estate sale has at least one pair. Every new operator overprices them by calculating full melt value on total weight. Now you know better.
