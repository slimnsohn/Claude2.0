## THE FUNDAMENTAL REALITY OF THIS CATEGORY

Costume jewelry has no intrinsic metal value. None. The silver-looking brooch is brass. The gold-looking bracelet is copper-plated. The "diamonds" are rhinestones. None of this matters for valuation purposes because **this category is 100% collector-driven** — and that changes everything.

The same rhinestone brooch is worth $5 or $500 depending entirely on who made it and whether it's signed. A Trifari brooch and a generic rhinestone brooch look nearly identical in a jewelry box. One is worth $5. One is worth $200–$4,200. The signature on the back of the piece is the entire ballgame.

**The non-negotiable rule: Every piece of costume jewelry gets flipped over and examined for a maker's signature before any piece is priced.** This takes 30 seconds per piece. Skipping it is how you put $1,500 worth of Miriam Haskell in the $5 bin.

## SECTION 1: HOW TO FIND SIGNATURES

Costume jewelry signatures are small and in unexpected locations. Always use your loupe.

**Where to look:**

| Piece type | Where to check |
|-----------|---------------|
| Brooch / pin | Back of piece, anywhere on the finding |
| Necklace | Inside the clasp mechanism — open it completely |
| Bracelet | Inside clasp, or on a small separate tag attached to the clasp |
| Earrings | Back of clip mechanism, or on the post finding |
| Pendant | Back of pendant body |
| Metal hang tag | Preserve these — a small oval or rectangular metal tag hanging from the clasp is almost always a Miriam Haskell or similar premium maker signature |

**Signature formats:**

- Stamped name in metal: TRIFARI, CORO, MONET, NAPIER, WEISS, EISENBERG, KRAMER
- Script signature: often Miriam Haskell (in script or block letters on oval or horseshoe tag)
- Crown symbol + TRIFARI: the Crown Trifari mark, often the most valuable Trifari pieces
- Copyright symbol (©): appears on mid-century American pieces after copyright laws were applied to jewelry designs — useful for dating
- Patent numbers: appear on earlier pieces (1920s–1940s)
- Country of origin marks: AUSTRIA, W. GERMANY, CZECHOSLOVAKIA — these are authentication clues, not maker marks

**No signature found:** Do not assume it's worthless. Many valuable early pieces are unsigned. Miriam Haskell didn't use permanent signature tags until the 1940s — earlier pieces are identified by construction style. Unsigned Bakelite can be worth $200+. But if you can't identify it: price conservatively as unsigned vintage ($5–$35).

## SECTION 2: THE DESIGNER TIER SYSTEM

### TIER 1 — PREMIUM DESIGNERS ($500–$5,000+)

**CHANEL**
Mark: CHANEL in block letters, or interlocked CC logo
The most valuable and most faked costume jewelry brand. Founded by Coco Chanel; continued producing costume lines after her death.
Authentication: Chanel marks are clean and crisp — perfectly symmetrical CC logos, sharp text edges. Fakes have uneven interlocking, fuzzy text, wrong proportions. Under loupe: real Chanel marks have clean, precise stamping.
Value examples (2024 auction results):
- Five-piece Chanel set (pearl choker + brooch + two pairs earrings): $2,772
- Unsigned Chanel pearl necklace (Lucite and pearls, attributed): $875
- Three Chanel velvet and gilt metal cuffs: $1,375
- Any authenticated Chanel piece: escalate before pricing
Floor price for authenticated Chanel: $300–$3,000+ per piece

**CHRISTIAN DIOR**
Mark: CHRISTIAN DIOR, Chr. DIOR, or CHR DIOR
Early Dior costume jewelry (1950s–1960s) commands the highest premiums.
Designer pieces (John Galliano, Raf Simons era) also valuable.
Value examples:
- Dior necklace by John Galliano (red glass beads): $4,400 in 2024
- Mid-century Dior Aurora Borealis rhinestone pieces: $200–$800
Floor price for authenticated Dior: $200–$4,400 per piece

**YVES SAINT LAURENT / YSL**
Mark: YSL interlocked logo, or YVES SAINT LAURENT
Strong and growing collector demand. 1970s–1980s pieces most collected.
Floor price: $200–$2,000+ per piece

**ELSA SCHIAPARELLI**
Mark: SCHIAPARELLI
Surrealist designs (1930s–1950s) — iconic in fashion history.
Aurora borealis rhinestones, unusual colors, lobster clasps, shoe motifs.
Value: $300–$5,000+ per piece. Any Schiaparelli: escalate.

**TRIFARI (signed Crown Trifari or early pieces)**
Mark: Crown symbol above TRIFARI in script or block letters; or TRIFARI alone; or KTF (early mark)
The crown mark indicates Alfred Philippe-designed pieces from the golden era (1930s–1960s). Philippe had worked at Cartier — these pieces reflect fine jewelry standards.
"Invisible settings" (stones set so the metal prongs are invisible from front): maximum premium.
Value examples:
- Poured glass Trifari bracelet: $4,200 (2023)
- Trifari enamel and rhinestone necklace: $3,600 (2024)
- Typical Crown Trifari brooch in good condition: $50–$250
- Rare Alfred Philippe pieces: $500–$4,200+
Value range: $40–$4,200 depending on piece type, era, and complexity

**MIRIAM HASKELL**
Mark: Script "Miriam Haskell" on oval or horseshoe-shaped metal plaque (tag), or block letters in cartouche; early pieces signed "H" on clasp only; pre-1940 pieces often unsigned (identified by construction)
Known for: Russian gold-tone wire, hand-wired seed pearls and baroque glass pearls, filigree medallions, intricate multi-strand constructions. Pieces feel substantial and complex — each bead individually hand-wired.
Dating: Oval/horseshoe signature tags = 1940s onward. Block letter tags = later. No tag = possibly pre-1940 (identify by construction style).
Value examples:
- Ruby crystal necklace + earrings set: $950 (2024)
- Colorful beaded necklace + earrings set: $2,400 (2023)
- Brass brooch with faux pearls and gold leaf: $250 (2024)
Value range: $75–$2,500+ depending on complexity, completeness, and condition

### TIER 2 — SOLID COLLECTOR VALUE ($50–$500)

**EISENBERG**
Mark: EISENBERG, EISENBERG ICE, or EISENBERG ORIGINAL
Founded 1914, Chicago. Commissioned Swarovski crystals; heavy, clear rhinestones in ornate settings.
Original pieces (pre-1945 Eisenberg Original): $150–$800+
Eisenberg Ice (post-WWII): $50–$250
Look for large, dramatic rhinestone pieces with exceptional sparkle.

**SCHREINER**
Mark: SCHREINER NEW YORK or SCHREINER
Inverted stones (stones set face-down to create distinctive glow), unusual color combinations.
Strong collector following among sophisticated costume collectors.
Value: $100–$600+

**HOBÉ**
Mark: HOBÉ in block letters or script (often inside clasp)
French-American family firm, intricate handmade pieces, often using semiprecious stones and elaborate wire work.
Value: $50–$400

**MARCEL BOUCHER**
Mark: M. BOUCHER, BOUCHER, or MB
High-quality rhinestone work, fine metalwork, often used sterling silver.
Value: $75–$500

**JOSEFF OF HOLLYWOOD**
Mark: JOSEFF or JOSEFF HOLLYWOOD
Made jewelry for Hollywood films (1930s–1950s). Russian gold matte finish is signature look.
Film provenance pieces: $200–$800+
Standard Joseff: $100–$400

**CINER**
Mark: CINER
Transitioned from fine jewelry to high-end costume during Depression. Early 1930s pieces in sterling. Later pieces consistently high quality.
Value: $50–$400

**KRAMER**
Mark: KRAMER, KRAMER OF NY, or KRAMER OF NEW YORK
New York rhinestone specialist. Frank Hess (from Miriam Haskell) designed "Amourelle" line.
Value: $40–$200. Kramer Austria (Austrian-made): higher premium.

**WEISS**
Mark: WEISS in block letters
Known for brilliant rhinestone pieces in unusual aurora borealis and colored stones.
Value: $50–$250

**HATTIE CARNEGIE**
Mark: HATTIE CARNEGIE
Important mid-century American designer, produced elegant pieces.
Value: $60–$350

**STANLEY HAGLER**
Mark: STANLEY HAGLER NYC or similar
Haskell-inspired work; later, independent designs. Intricate, hand-wired constructions.
Value: $75–$500

### UNSIGNED COSTUME JEWELRY

Unsigned vintage costume jewelry has value only when:
1. The materials are distinctive and identifiable (Bakelite, poured glass, early Lucite)
2. The craftsmanship is exceptional and era-specific (Art Deco geometric, hand-wired construction matching Haskell style)
3. The piece is a complete matching set in excellent condition

**General unsigned vintage pricing:**
- Individual rhinestone brooch: $5–$20
- Rhinestone necklace: $8–$35
- Matching necklace + earrings set, excellent: $20–$65
- Complete parure (necklace + bracelet + earrings + brooch), excellent: $40–$120

## SECTION 3: BAKELITE — THE UNSIGNED HIGH-VALUE EXCEPTION

Bakelite (1907–1940s) is pre-war thermosetting plastic that commands strong collector prices even completely unsigned. It was replaced by cheaper plastics post-WWII and is no longer produced.

### How to Identify Bakelite

**The hot water test:** Run piece under hot water for 30 seconds or rub vigorously with thumb to generate heat. Genuine Bakelite produces a distinctive, unmistakable formaldehyde-like or camphor smell when warm. Nothing else smells quite like it. This test is definitive.

**The appearance test:**
- Heavier and denser than modern plastic
- Slightly warm to the touch (vs. cool like glass)
- Often slightly translucent in thin sections when held to strong light
- Colors: cherry red, butterscotch yellow, forest green, black, creamy white, marbled/swirled patterns

**The feel test:**
- Smooth and substantial
- Vintage pieces often have a patina from age and handling

**What is NOT Bakelite:**
- Lucite (clear or translucent acrylic): no smell, lighter
- Celluloid (pre-Bakelite): flammable; different smell (camphor)
- Modern resin reproductions: no smell, different weight

### Bakelite Value by Type

| Type | Value range | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|
| Simple bangles, common colors | $20–$75 | Very common |
| Bangles in rare colors (red, marbled) | $75–$200 | Color drives value |
| Figural pins (fruit, animals, people) | $50–$400+ | Subject and complexity matter |
| Carved Bakelite bangles | $75–$300 | Intricate carving adds premium |
| Complete sets (bangle + earrings) | $100–$500+ | Sets always command premium |
| Laminated/marbled patterns | $100–$400 | Rarer; high collector demand |
| Unusual colors or patterns | $150–$600+ | Orange, deep purple, unusual marbled |

**Bakelite fakes exist:** Modern resin reproductions. The hot water/friction test settles it. Reproduction Bakelite produces no smell.

## SECTION 4: ERA IDENTIFICATION — DATING CLUES FROM CONSTRUCTION

Era identification helps price unsigned pieces and verify authenticity of signed ones.

### Clasp/Finding Types by Era

**C-clasp (simple curved C, no safety mechanism):**
Pre-1910. The pin catches inside the curved C — no closure. Any piece with a C-clasp is authentically old.

**Trombone clasp (push-pull barrel mechanism):**
1900–1940. Common on Art Nouveau and early Art Deco brooches.

**Pot-metal or white metal findings (pre-WWII):**
1920s–1940s findings are often white metal (zinc alloy). Heavier, slightly rough on back.

**Safety catch (rotating closure on pin):**
1910 onward. Standard on most mid-century and later pieces.

**Locking C-clasp:**
1950s onward. More secure version of original C.

**Roller catch:**
Post-1955 mainly. Very secure mechanism.

### Stone Setting Types by Era

**Open-back settings (pre-1940):** Stone visible from back of setting — older technique.
**Closed-back settings (pre-1920):** Metal completely covers back — very old.
**Prong settings:** Consistent across eras; quality of prong work indicates period.
**Glued stones:** Post-1950s mainly; mass market pieces; reduces value.
**Hand-wired stones (Haskell style):** Labor-intensive; pre-1960s mainly.
**Invisible settings (Crown Trifari):** Stones sit in channel with no visible prongs from front — rare, premium.

### Copyright Symbols

**© symbol on costume jewelry:** Courts extended copyright protection to jewelry designs in 1955. Any piece marked © was made 1955 or later. This is a useful floor for dating.

**"Pat. Pend." or patent numbers:** Pre-1955 primarily. Helps date to earlier era.

## SECTION 5: CONDITION ASSESSMENT FOR COSTUME JEWELRY

Costume jewelry condition sensitivity is extreme — there is no melt floor to catch damaged pieces.

### The Condition Hierarchy

**EXCELLENT:** All rhinestones present and bright; no missing stones; original finish intact; clasp works perfectly; no green patina or base metal showing. Full value.

**GOOD:** One or two missing stones; minor tarnishing; clasp functional. Reduce 20–35%.

**FAIR:** Multiple missing stones; significant tarnishing; finish worn in areas; clasp needs repair. Reduce 50–70%.

**POOR:** Heavy stone loss; major finish damage; non-functional clasp; base metal visible. Decorative only: 10–20% of excellent price.

### The Stone Rules

**Missing rhinestones:** Finding exact replacement stones for signed costume jewelry is difficult and expensive. Buyers know this. Each missing stone reduces value 10–20%.

**Replaced or wrong stones:** Amateur repairs with wrong-color or wrong-cut stones actually reduce value more than missing stones — the alteration damages authenticity.

**Cloudy or scratched stones:** Rhinestones scratch and cloud with age. Bright, clear stones = higher value. Cloudy stones reduce value 15–25%.

### The Finish Rules

**Rhodium plating (silver-looking finish):** Wears through to brass/copper on corners and high points. Heavy wear-through: significant reduction.

**Gold-tone plating:** Same issue. Look for greenish or reddish base metal showing through.

**Green patina on findings:** Common on brass-based pieces. Significant patina: reduces value 20–40%.

**Enamel:** Check for chips, crazing, or loss. Enamel damage is irreparable for most pieces.

### Do Not Clean Before Pricing

A critical rule: **never clean, polish, or attempt to restore costume jewelry before pricing.** The wrong cleaner destroys rhinestone coatings, removes period patina, and strips plating. Buyers who collect signed costume jewelry expect age-appropriate patina and know how to clean their purchases properly. A well-intentioned cleaning can reduce value 30–60%.

## SECTION 6: SET COMPLETION PREMIUM

Costume jewelry sets multiply in value dramatically when complete:

| Configuration | Value multiplier vs. individual |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Single piece | 1.0x baseline |
| Necklace + earrings (demi-parure) | 2.5–3.5x individual earrings |
| Necklace + bracelet + earrings | 3.5–5x individual piece |
| Full parure (all 4: necklace + bracelet + earrings + brooch) | 5–8x individual piece |

**Always inventory the full jewelry box before pricing any piece.** Eight individual pieces might generate $80 total. The same eight pieces as a complete Kramer parure: $250–$400.

## SECTION 7: THE CHANNEL DECISION

### SELL ON FLOOR ($5–$65):
- All unsigned rhinestone and generic pieces
- Common signed pieces: Coro, Sarah Coventry, Monet, Napier, BSK, Avon
- Bakelite bangles in common colors under $40 floor price
- Any piece in fair or poor condition regardless of signature

### LIST ONLINE (eBay, Etsy, or Ruby Lane):
- Any Tier 2 designer piece in good+ condition: Eisenberg, Hobé, Marcel Boucher, Weiss, Joseff, Kramer, Ciner
- Any complete parure in a signed designer
- Unusual Bakelite (figural, carved, rare colors)
- Any complete signed set where the parure premium makes online worthwhile

### ESCALATE (do not self-price):
- Any authenticated Chanel piece
- Any Schiaparelli piece
- Any early Christian Dior piece
- Any poured glass Trifari with complex construction
- Any complete Miriam Haskell parure
- Any single Miriam Haskell piece where the construction suggests high complexity or early era

## SECTION 8: THE 5 MOST EXPENSIVE MISTAKES IN THIS CATEGORY

**Mistake 1: The $5 bin with a Miriam Haskell tag**
A beaded necklace with a small oval metal tag reading "Miriam Haskell" placed in the $5 costume pile. The tag is the signature — it's the piece's identity. A Haskell necklace in good condition: $200–$2,400 depending on complexity. The oval tag is the entire tell. Look for it on every necklace clasp.

**Mistake 2: Pricing a poured glass Trifari as generic rhinestone**
A Trifari bracelet with stones that look slightly milky or three-dimensional — "poured glass" rather than flat rhinestones. Priced at $15 as generic vintage. A poured glass Trifari bracelet sold for $4,200 in 2023. The Crown Trifari mark is on the clasp. Check every clasp.

**Mistake 3: Passing on unsigned Bakelite as "old plastic"**
A heavy, dark red bangle that smells like formaldehyde when rubbed — priced at $3 as "old plastic jewelry." Genuine cherry red Bakelite bangle: $75–$200. The hot water/friction smell test is definitive and takes 30 seconds.

**Mistake 4: Breaking up a complete parure**
Four matching pieces in a velvet tray — necklace, bracelet, brooch, earrings — all signed Eisenberg. Priced individually at $25 each ($100 total). As a complete signed Eisenberg parure: $200–$600. Always look at the whole tray before pricing any piece.

**Mistake 5: Cleaning a signed piece before the sale**
A Miriam Haskell brooch with some patina on the Russian gold-tone finding, "cleaned up" with brass polish before pricing. The polish strips the period finish that collectors specifically value. The piece that would have sold for $180 now looks wrong and sells for $45. Never clean signed costume jewelry.

## SECTION 9: QUICK REFERENCE — IN THE FIELD

### 60-Second Triage for Every Piece
1. Flip it over immediately
2. Use loupe — check every surface for signature
3. Signature found? → Identify tier, adjust price accordingly
4. No signature? → Check for Bakelite (friction/smell test)
5. No Bakelite? → Check for exceptional construction (hand-wired, unusual materials)
6. Price based on tier and condition
