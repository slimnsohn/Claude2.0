User Guide

The Synthetic Population Engine generates statistically realistic AI individuals from real survey data and polls them on questions. Demographics, politics, religion, and income bracket come from a real CES respondent. Secondary lifestyle fields (marital status, occupation, household size, commute) are plausibility-filled at build time based on that respondent's demographic profile. Every opinion is grounded in real survey answers.

Server offline — static docs mode.

1. How it works

The engine has three interlocking layers: a population sampled from real survey data, a KNN opinion model that derives each persona's baseline views from their demographic neighbors in the source survey, and a belief layer that lets those views shift in response to real news headlines — bounded so personas evolve without becoming different people.

Population layer

The source is the 2024 Cooperative Election Study (CES), which interviewed roughly 60,000 Americans on their demographics and political views. From those 60,000 records, the engine draws a representative sample of 5,000 personas using raking (iterative proportional fitting) — a technique that reweights the draw so the final set matches census targets on sex, race, education, and age. Party identification and urban/rural targets come from CES's own commonweight post-stratification weight rather than the raw panel counts — the raw CES opt-in panel skews Democratic, and weighting corrects the population's D−R margin from +13.6 (unweighted) to +1.9. The weighted party targets are strong_dem 21.9%, strong_rep 20.1%, independent 18.7%, with the remaining leaner/weak-partisan groups around 9–10% each. Personas are grouped into 134 demographic archetypes for efficient polling. Each persona stores the ces_row_id of the real respondent it was drawn from, so every opinion trace is ultimately grounded in a real person's data.

KNN opinion model

When a poll question arrives, the engine keyword-matches it to one of 27 verified CES survey columns covering presidential and Congress approval, the economy and inflation, immigration, healthcare, environment and climate, guns, abortion, student debt, and Ukraine (see the FAQ for the full list). Every column mapping has been verified against the official CES 2024 codebook and empirically cross-tabbed against party ID in the raw data. For each persona, the engine finds the 50 most demographically similar real CES respondents (the K-nearest neighbors) and reads their actual answers to that column. Those answers become a probability distribution over yes / no / unsure. The LLM direction used in belief scoring is a signed strength from -1 to +1; the keyword fallback uses ±0.5 or 0. Questions that don't match any covered column are blocked — the engine never invents an answer from scratch. Questions phrased as a negation ("do you oppose…", "do you disapprove…") are also rejected with an error asking you to rephrase affirmatively, because the matcher would otherwise silently return the support distribution for an oppose question.

One documented proxy: CES 2024 was fielded in November 2024 while Biden was in office, so it contains no direct Trump job-approval item. "Do you approve of Trump's job performance?" is answered using each matched respondent's actual 2024 presidential vote (CC24_410) as a stand-in. This is disclosed in the column registry and validates within ~4 points of the current real-poll average, but it is a vote proxy, not a measured approval rating.

Belief layer

On top of the KNN baseline, each persona carries a beliefs dictionary: per-topic bounded shifts in the range ±0.15 that tilt the probability distribution before sampling. These shifts accumulate from news cycles and decay back toward zero (the CES baseline) with a 14-day half-life, so quiet news periods mean gradual reversion to type.

2. Walkthrough: run a poll

Enter a question

Open the Poll tab. Type a yes/no question in the text field — for example, "Do you approve of Trump's job performance?" or "Do you support building a border wall?" — and click Run Poll. Coverage is checked when the poll runs. If the question matches a CES column the poll proceeds; if it does not match, you will see a message reading "This question isn't covered by CES survey data" after you click. There is no pre-run indicator.

Why blocking matters: an unanswered question is honest. A fabricated answer dressed up as demographically calibrated data is misleading. If your question is blocked, try rephrasing it toward a covered topic. Good examples that pass the matcher: "Do you approve of Trump's job performance?", "Is the economy getting better or worse?", "Do you support increasing border patrol on the US-Mexico border?", "Do you support background checks on all gun sales?", "Do you support allowing abortion as a matter of choice?"

Phrase questions affirmatively (negation rule)

Questions phrased as a negation — "do you oppose the border wall?", "do you disapprove of Trump?", "are you against banning assault rifles?" — are rejected with an error (HTTP 400) before any poll runs. The underlying keyword matcher is polarity-blind: it would match "do you oppose building a border wall?" to the border-wall column and report the support distribution as "yes", silently inverting the answer. Rather than guess, the engine refuses and asks you to rephrase affirmatively: "Do you support X?". The engine answers support/approval distributions. Words that are part of the proposal itself — "ban assault rifles", "make abortion illegal", "repeal the ACA" — are fine; only negated question stems are blocked.

Demographic filters

You can narrow the poll to a subset of the population before running it. Filters are available for state, party identification, race, education level, and age bracket — each accepts a single value. Stacking multiple filters narrows the cohort further. A live count below the filter panel shows how many profiles match the current selection before you run.

Reading the Results tab

After a poll runs, switch to the Results tab. The top section shows the overall distribution (yes / no / unsure percentages) with a 95% bootstrap confidence interval computed over archetype-level responses — one representative per archetype, weighted by archetype size. Below that, demographic breakdowns appear by party, race, age group, and education; these show distributions only and do not carry their own per-group confidence intervals.

Confidence intervals are wide on small cohorts. A ±12% interval on a 40-persona filtered poll tells you the underlying data is thin; interpret those numbers with caution. The full 5,000-persona population typically yields confidence intervals in the ±2–4% range.

Past polls are listed on the Poll tab under "Recent Polls". The Backtest tab is for running polls against named population snapshots.

3. Walkthrough: update the population (news cycle)

What a cycle does, end to end

A news cycle has five stages, all automated:

  1. Fetch headlines — RSS feeds from AP, NPR, BBC, Reuters, and Google News are scraped for current stories. The engine collects up to 10 headlines per feed.
  2. Score stories — if the environment variable ANTHROPIC_API_KEY is set, a single batched call to Claude Haiku tags each headline with relevant topics, direction (a signed strength from -1 to +1), salience (how likely the story reaches a typical news consumer), and outlet-family framing (right-leaning, left-leaning, mainstream). Without the API key the engine falls back to a keyword scorer covering 10 belief topics (economy, trump_approval, immigration, healthcare, climate, fiscal, education, crime, foreign_policy, social) using ±0.5 or 0 direction values, without framing nuance.
  3. Simulate exposure — each persona probabilistically encounters stories based on its assigned news source. A persona tagged fox_news gets the right-family framing; msnbc, npr, nyt personas get left-family framing; abc and local_tv personas get mainstream framing. Confirmation bias is modeled: counter-attitudinal news moves partisans only 40% as much as congruent news, and strong partisans are 30% less movable overall.
  4. Apply belief shifts — each exposure that reaches a persona nudges its per-topic belief by a small amount. All shifts are bounded to ±0.15 and every change is appended to the persona's drift_log with a date, topic, delta, update_id (the cycle id), and shift_after. Beliefs decay toward the CES baseline with a 14-day half-life, so a persona that stops seeing news on a topic will gradually revert. A persona's drift_log is viewable in the Population view profile panel (raw JSON).
  5. Calibrate — after beliefs are updated, two anchor questions ("Do you approve of Trump's job performance?" and "Is the economy getting better or worse?") are re-polled via archetype-representative weighting and compared to real poll numbers stored in data/benchmarks.json. See Section 5 for what happens next.

Three ways to run a cycle

  1. Events tab button — the "Run Update Cycle" button on the Events tab triggers POST /api/world-updates/cycle. Results appear in the drift chart on the same tab when the call returns. This is the easiest method for manual one-off runs.
  2. update.bat — a batch file at the project root. Double-click it or run it from a terminal. Useful for testing outside the browser or calling from a scheduler.
  3. python run_update_cycle.py — the underlying Python entry point. Run from the project root with the virtual environment active. Accepts no flags; exits 0 on success, non-zero on error.

Scheduling with Windows Task Scheduler

For daily automated cycles, create a Task Scheduler entry:

  1. Open Task Scheduler → Create Basic Task.
  2. Name it (e.g., "SynPop Update Cycle"), set trigger to Daily at your preferred time.
  3. For the Action, choose Start a program. In the Program/script field, enter the full path to update.bat, for example:
    C:\Users\slims\Desktop\Claude 2.0\apps\synthetic-population\update.bat
  4. In Start in, enter the project directory (same folder as update.bat).
  5. Click Finish. The task will run once daily, updating beliefs and logging calibration results.

Reading the drift chart and drift_log

The Events tab shows a drift chart plotting aggregate belief movement per cycle across topics. An upward bar on "economy" means the population collectively shifted toward a more positive economic view during that cycle; a downward bar means sentiment worsened. The chart is built from data/belief_history.json.

To inspect a single persona's history, open the Population tab, click a persona row to open the detail panel, and scroll to the Drift Log section. Each entry records a date, the topic affected, the delta magnitude, the update_id (cycle id), and the shift_after value.

4. Walkthrough: increase the population

The default population is 5,000 personas. You can rebuild it at any size with: 

python build_population.py --target-n 5000

Replace 5000 with any target count. Larger populations give tighter confidence intervals but take longer to poll. The CES source has 60,000 respondents, so targets up to around 50,000 are reasonable; above that you will exhaust unique source rows.

Reading the balance report

After sampling, the builder prints a distribution-vs-target report to the console comparing the achieved proportions against census targets for sex, race, education, and age, plus CES-native targets for party and urban/rural status. A truncated example: 

race_h:
  white                  58.0%  target= 58.0%  OK
  black                  12.0%  target= 12.0%  OK
  hispanic               19.1%  target= 19.0%  OK
  ...

Max marginal gap: 2.2%

The ±3% balance gate

If any demographic dimension is more than 3 percentage points off target, the builder aborts without writing the registry. This prevents a silently imbalanced population from polluting future polls. When the gate fires, the console explains which dimension failed and by how much. Adjust --target-n (sometimes a different N clears the gate) or investigate the source data.

The gate is a safeguard, not a tuning target. If you find yourself repeatedly tweaking N to squeak past the gate, step back and investigate whether the CES source file is complete and correctly formatted.

Backups and restore

Before writing a new registry, the builder copies the current data/profiles/registry.json to data/profiles/registry.backup.<timestamp>.json. To restore a previous population, copy the backup file over registry.json: 

copy data\profiles\registry.backup.20260612000436.json data\profiles\registry.json

The build report for the new population is written to data/profiles/build_report.json and is also shown on the Data Sources tab in the app.

5. Walkthrough: keep it calibrated

What calibration is for

Calibration is a diagnostic check, not a tuning mechanism. After each news cycle the engine re-polls the population on two politically salient anchor questions and compares the results to real poll numbers you supply. The goal is to catch runaway drift early — if the synthetic population diverges badly from reality, the system flags it and dampens further shifts rather than silently producing misleading output.

Refreshing real numbers

The Benchmark tab has a Compare button for each question. Clicking it runs the synthetic poll against the real results you supply and saves a last_comparison record — useful for diagnostics. For a new question the comparison is saved with the real results you entered; for an existing question only last_comparison is updated — the stored real_results and date are not changed.

To truly refresh the anchor numbers that drive calibration, you must edit data/benchmarks.json directly: find the entry for the question and update its real_results object and date field (format: YYYY-MM-DD), then save the file. The next cycle will compare against those new values. A future UI improvement to support in-app anchor editing is planned.

Benchmark numbers are never scraped automatically. Scraping introduces silent staleness, site-breakage errors, and terms-of-service risk. You do the lookup; the engine does the comparison.

Badge meanings

The Events tab shows a calibration badge after each cycle with one of four verdicts:

  • PASS — mean absolute error (MAE) is ≤ 5% on all anchor questions. The population is well-aligned. No action required.
  • DRIFT WARNING — at least one anchor question has MAE > 5%. All belief shifts for this cycle are automatically halved ("dampening") and the event is logged in drift_log. The population continues to function but is being pulled back toward reality. If drift warnings persist across several cycles, refresh your benchmark numbers or investigate whether recent news scoring is overcorrecting on a particular topic.
  • STALE — your benchmark numbers are more than 30 days old. The engine cannot issue a pass or fail because the comparison target is too old to be meaningful. No dampening occurs, but no pass is claimed either. Open the Benchmark tab and refresh with current poll numbers.
  • NONE — no cycle has run yet, or calibration history is empty.

Calibration history

Every calibration result is appended to data/calibration_history.json with the cycle timestamp, per-anchor MAEs, the verdict, and whether dampening was applied. This file gives you a longitudinal view of how well the synthetic population tracks reality over time.

6. Other operations

Snapshots and backtesting

The Backtest tab lets you freeze a copy of the current population as a named snapshot. Future polls can be run against a snapshot to compare how opinions have shifted between two points in time. A snapshot stores a full copy of all persona profiles (demographics, beliefs, and drift_log) at the moment it is created. Snapshots are stored under data/snapshots/.

Demographic poll filters

Filters are applied before a poll runs, not after. You can combine: state, party (Democrat / Republican / Independent), race, education tier, and age bracket. Each filter accepts a single value. The filter panel shows a live match count so you know the effective sample size before you run.

Toggling individual world updates

The Events tab lists every scored headline in the current cycle. Each row has a toggle switch. Turning a story off removes it from the active-shifts calculation without deleting it — useful for excluding an outlier story you believe was scored incorrectly. Toggling is immediate and persisted to data/world_updates.json.

7. FAQ & limitations

Why was my question rejected for "negated phrasing"?

Questions phrased "do you oppose / disapprove / are you against X" are rejected with a 400 error on every poll path (Poll tab, Benchmark compare, Polymarket ask). The keyword matcher is polarity-blind — it would match your question to the right topic but return the support distribution as "yes", inverting the answer with full confidence and no warning. Rephrase affirmatively: "Do you support X?". The engine answers support/approval distributions; compute the oppose share yourself from the "no" column.

How accurate is it? (the validation study)

A blind validation study (VALIDATION.md) compared the population's answers to real polls: synthetic distributions were recorded and committed before real numbers were looked up. Two rounds were run — round one exposed that most of the original CES column mappings were wrong; the registry was rebuilt against the official CES 2024 codebook (30/30 empirical direction checks) and the study repeated. After the registry correction and the commonweight-weighted population rebuild, typical error on identity-anchored questions is single digits. The honest framing: the population is good at culture/politics support questions — stable, identity-anchored opinions where demographics and real survey microdata carry the signal — and weak at fast-moving sentiment levels. The CES baseline is frozen at November 2024; real opinion on things like border enforcement has moved since, and the belief layer closes that gap only slowly (its shifts are deliberately bounded). Two caveats on the worst rows: the Ukraine items are CES multi-select ("check all that apply"), which suppresses selection — treat those numbers as floors, not levels; and the Medicaid-expansion "real" number is itself a derived estimate (no pollster fields a direct national question), so the true miss may be smaller than it looks. Even where the level is off, the population almost always gets the majority side and the partisan ordering right.

Has the system been independently audited?

Yes — an adversarial integrity audit (AUDIT.md) actively tried to prove the system cheats. Verdict on integrity: clean. Real poll numbers can never tune opinions — the only code path where they touch the engine is a disclosed dampening gate that, when synthetic drift diverges from reality, halves all belief shifts (×0.5), shrinking them toward the CES baseline, never toward the real poll value. No fitted constants or optimization against benchmarks were found, and the blind-validation protocol was verified against git commit timestamps. The audit also found two high-severity bugs in the poll path — an archetype-ID mismatch that corrupted filtered polls, and the negation blindness described above — both since fixed (the negation gate now rejects negated questions). It additionally flagged that the population's party mix baked in the raw CES panel's Democratic skew; the builder now rakes party targets to CES's own commonweight, moving the D−R margin from +13.6 to +1.9.

Why is belief drift bounded at ±0.15?

The bound keeps personas grounded in their CES-derived baseline. Without it, a sustained string of one-sided news cycles could push a persona's views so far from their measured starting point that the demographic anchor becomes meaningless — the persona would no longer represent the survey respondent it was drawn from. The ±0.15 cap means the most extreme total shift on any topic is still within the range of plausible opinion movement for a real person, while the 14-day decay ensures that "moved" personas return to baseline when news quiets down.

Why real data only? Can I add hypothetical personas?

The engine is designed as a measurement instrument, not a simulation sandbox. Every inference about the synthetic population's opinions derives its validity from the fact that the personas are grounded in real respondents and real answers. Injecting hypothetical personas breaks that chain. If you need to explore a demographic profile not well-represented in the CES, the right approach is to find a real survey source that covers it — not to invent respondents.

What does "stale" actually mean, and why not just scrape current numbers?

"Stale" means your benchmark reference values are more than 30 days old. Opinion on salient political topics can shift 5–10 points in a month during high-news periods, so a benchmark from six weeks ago may no longer represent current public opinion. Scraping is not used because (a) poll aggregator sites change their markup without notice, breaking scrapers silently; (b) different polls use different question wording and sampling frames — a human choosing a benchmark is making a methodological judgment a scraper cannot replicate; and (c) you should know which poll you are comparing against so the calibration verdict is interpretable.

How many topics does the engine actually cover?

27 verified CES 2024 common-content columns, every mapping checked against the official codebook and empirically cross-tabbed against party ID (verify_ces_mappings.py, 30/30 direction checks). Grouped by theme:

  • Approval: Trump job approval (CC24_410 — a documented proxy: CES 2024 was fielded in November 2024 under Biden, so no direct Trump-approval item exists; each matched respondent's actual 2024 presidential vote stands in for approval), Biden approval (CC24_312a, historical), Congress approval (CC24_312b), Harris approval (CC24_312i, historical)
  • Economy: economy retrospective (CC24_301), household income past year (CC24_302), prices/inflation past year (CC24_303)
  • Immigration: legal status for tax-paying immigrants (CC24_323a), increase border patrol (CC24_323b), build a border wall (CC24_323c), pathway to citizenship for Dreamers (CC24_323d)
  • Healthcare: Medicaid work requirements (CC24_328c), repeal the ACA (CC24_328d), expand Medicaid (CC24_328e)
  • Environment: EPA regulate CO2 (CC24_326a), renewable energy mandate (CC24_326b), increase fossil-fuel production (CC24_326d), halt new federal oil and gas leases (CC24_326e)
  • Guns: ban assault rifles (CC24_321a), easier concealed-carry permits (CC24_321b), universal background checks (CC24_321c)
  • Abortion: abortion as a matter of choice (CC24_324a), total abortion ban (CC24_324c), expand abortion access (CC24_324d)
  • Education: forgive up to $20,000 in student loan debt (CC24_323f — misnumbered into the immigration grid in the released data; codebook-confirmed)
  • Foreign policy: provide arms to Ukraine (CC24_308a_4), stay out of Ukraine (CC24_308a_1) — both are multi-select "check all that apply" items, so the numbers are floors, not direct support levels

Not covered (the engine returns "not covered" honestly — these items simply do not exist in CES 2024): Medicare for All, $15 minimum wage, raising taxes on income over $400k, increasing deportations, a carbon tax, and tariffs. Earlier versions of this guide listed several of these as covered; that was a column-mapping bug, fixed in the registry correction.

Any question whose keywords match one of these columns is eligible for a poll; all others are blocked.

What happens if the CES source file is missing?

The opinion engine will not load at startup. Polls will return an error indicating CES data is unavailable. The population can still be browsed and world updates can still be toggled, but no new poll responses can be generated. Place the CES 2024 common-content CSV at data/raw/ces/ces_2024_common.csv and restart the server.

8. Walkthrough: Polymarket questions

The Polymarket tab puts the synthetic population side by side with real prediction markets. Click Refresh Markets and it fetches the top ~20 open markets by 24-hour volume from Polymarket's public Gamma API (no API key required). Each market row shows the market's implied YES% (the current price of the Yes outcome), its 24h volume, and a coverage badge: green means the population can answer the market's question from real CES data; grey means no CES coverage. Coverage uses a stricter keyword threshold than the regular poll matcher (a summed match score of at least 10), so a bare mention of "trump" in an unrelated market does not count as coverage. The most recent fetch is cached to data/polymarket_cache.json and served as a fallback when the Gamma API is unreachable.

For any covered market, Ask population runs the synthetic poll (10 runs, averaged to reduce sampling noise) and shows the population's yes / no / unsure split next to the market's implied YES% with the delta between them. The CES column that actually answered the question is named in the result, so you always know what was measured. There is also a free-form box at the top for any market-style question — it goes through the same coverage check, the same negation gate (rephrase "will X fail" / "do you oppose" questions affirmatively), and the same averaged synthetic poll.

What the numbers mean — read this before comparing. The population answers what the public supports or believes — not what will happen. A "Will X win?" market prices event probability: candidate quality, procedural rules, insider information, timing. The synthetic population knows none of that; it offers a public-opinion lens on the same question. The two numbers measure different things, and a gap between them is not automatically an edge. The lens is most meaningful for culture/politics markets where public sentiment is the question.

Best and worst uses

  • Best: markets about public support, approval levels, or cultural acceptance — "Will Trump's approval be above 45%?", markets keyed to abortion, guns, immigration, or healthcare sentiment. These map directly onto what the population measures, and validation shows single-digit error on exactly these identity-anchored questions.
  • Worst: event outcomes ("Will the bill pass by July?"), deadlines, sports, anything resolved by facts rather than opinion. The population has no model of events — only of people.

Also keep the validation caveats from Section 7 in mind here: fast-moving sentiment levels (e.g. border enforcement) lag reality because the CES baseline is frozen at November 2024, and Ukraine items are multi-select floors. Direction and partisan ordering are reliable; exact levels on fast-moving topics are not.
